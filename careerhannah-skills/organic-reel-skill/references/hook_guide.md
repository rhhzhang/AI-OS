# Hook Guide

## What a Hook Needs to Do

A hook has one job: make the specific person this reel is for stop scrolling. That person is a smart, ambitious professional — probably in finance, tech, consulting, or a startup. They have high standards, they've seen a thousand generic career advice reels, and they will not stop for anything that feels generic, predictable, or like it could have been said by anyone.

A strong hook does one of three things:
1. **Makes them see themselves** ("that's me")
2. **Surprises them with a counterintuitive claim** ("wait, what?")
3. **Promises specific value they don't have yet** ("I need to know that")

A weak hook does none of these. It states a broad truth everyone already knows, asks a question they can ignore, or opens with something that could apply to any creator's audience.

## Hook Types That Work For Hannah

### 1. The Mirror Hook
The viewer reads it and thinks "that's me." Uses specific details that feel personally addressed.

The best mirror hooks use a chain: [identity] + [situation] + [feeling or problem]. The longer and more specific the chain, the more powerfully it stops the right person.

**Examples from Hannah's content:**
- "If you are a conventionally successful person, you probably have mimetic ambition." — stops anyone who identifies as conventionally successful, immediately curious
- "If you are a high achieving person who went to a great school, got a high paying job, you're a top performer — but you still feel like you're not good enough..." — very long chain, very specific audience, extremely high resonance for that audience
- "if you're a smart ambitious person looking for a job, here are 5 places to find interesting roles that aren't LinkedIn" — direct mirror + immediate promise of specific value

**How to write one:** Start with the audience identity, add the specific situation they're in, add the tension or problem they feel. Don't rush to the punchline — let the mirror do its work first.

### 2. The Counterintuitive Claim
States something that contradicts what the viewer assumes. Creates cognitive dissonance that demands resolution. The viewer has to keep watching to find out why you're right.

**Examples:**
- "The job market is so cooked, you might as well build a billion-dollar company." — reframes a negative as an opportunity
- "The bullshit job recession is turning highly ambitious corporate employees into high-earning creators." — sounds like bad news for corporate workers but the reel inverts it
- "Elite firms didn't hire you because you're talented. They hired you because you're talented and you feel like you're not good enough." — direct contradiction of what they believe

**How to write one:** Take the conventional wisdom and flip it. Or take two things people don't associate and connect them. The surprise has to be defensible — you need to actually make good on it in the reel.

### 3. The Specific Value Promise
Names exactly what the viewer will walk away with. Works best for list and resource reels. No curiosity gap needed — the specificity is enough.

**Examples:**
- "here are 5 places to find interesting roles that aren't LinkedIn" — specific number + specific format + specific qualifier (not LinkedIn)
- "3 things I vibe coded as a nontechnical person who doesn't know how to code" — specific number + specific claimed context (nontechnical) that earns credibility

**How to write one:** Name the number, name what they'll get, name who it's for. The more specific each element, the stronger the hook.

### 4. The Sequel Hook
References a prior viral piece and adds nuance or corrects a misread. Earns credibility from the prior hit. Best used when Hannah has content that exploded and she wants to build on it.

**Examples:**
- "I made a video about why you should be low status but high income to build wealth and got over half a million views, but I'm afraid a lot of you got the wrong idea." — creates tension (you misunderstood my viral video), earns credibility (half a million views), promises the correction

**How to write one:** Reference the original, acknowledge what it accomplished, name the thing it left out or the misread that happened. The sequel hook only works if the original actually performed.

### 5. The Coined Term Reveal
Introduces or claims a term that names something the audience already experiences but has never had a word for. The naming itself is the value.

**Examples:**
- "The great bullshit job recession is here and there's never been a better time to become nonlinear." — owns the term, states it boldly
- "The bullshit job recession is turning highly ambitious corporate employees into high-earning creators." — presupposes the audience knows the term (or makes them want to know it)

**How to write one:** Name the phenomenon, own it, connect it to an outcome the audience cares about.

## Hook Rules

**Preserve Hannah's hook if she gives one.** If she came up with a hook and it passes the basic tests above, use it verbatim. Her instincts about what stops her specific audience are almost always right. Do not "improve" it without explicit permission.

**If the hook is weak**, write the full script using her hook AND present an alternative. Never just replace it.

**Never open with a question.** Questions let the viewer opt out. Declarative statements don't.

**Specificity beats cleverness.** A specific claim about a real number or a named institution beats a clever turn of phrase every time.

**Length is not a weakness.** The mirror hook often works by being long and specific. Don't cut it short to make it punchier — the specificity IS the punch.

## Hooks That Flopped (and Why)

These are the types of hooks that have been flagged as flat or generic in real feedback:

❌ "You probably think you got hired because of your achievements." — starts with "you probably," which is weak; "achievements" is vague
❌ "If you got into an elite firm — Goldman, McKinsey, top law — you probably think you got hired because of your achievements." — better but "probably think" is hedging, and "achievements" is still vague. The rewrite that works: "Elite firms didn't hire you because you're talented. They hired you because you're talented and you feel like you're not good enough."
❌ "Most people optimize for the wrong quadrant." — too abstract, no identity, no specific person
❌ "There are four types of wealth. Most people only talk about one." — listicle framing, not specific enough to stop the right person

The pattern: these fail because they don't name the specific person, the specific situation, or the specific counterintuitive claim clearly enough to create friction.
