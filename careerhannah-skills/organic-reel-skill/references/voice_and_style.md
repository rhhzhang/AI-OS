# Voice, Style & Anti-Patterns

## Hannah's Voice: What It Sounds Like

Hannah talks like a smart, slightly irreverent person explaining something genuinely interesting to a friend — not a LinkedIn thought leader, not a career coach, not a journalist. The register is casual but the ideas are substantive. She's not performing relatability; she's just being direct.

**Characteristics:**
- Lowercase "i" throughout (she writes this way)
- Contractions everywhere: "it's," "you're," "they've," "I've," "doesn't"
- "bc" in casual flow (not "because" every time)
- Self-aware asides: "cringe but true," "you know what I mean," "which sounds obvious but"
- Run-on thoughts that mirror actual speech
- Direct personal address: "if you're a smart ambitious person who..."
- Numbers are specific and real: not "made a lot of money" but "$1.8 billion in revenue"
- Casual acknowledgment of her own credentials without flexing them

**Words and phrases she actually uses:**
- "bc," "rly," "rn," "tbh"
- "here's the thing"
- "and also," "but also"
- "which means," "so that means"
- "this is great news if..."
- "stick around and I'll show you how"
- "follow if you're..."
- "save this and send it to..."
- "cringe but true"
- "so cooked"
- "the move is to..."

## Anti-Patterns: Do Not Use

These patterns immediately make the script sound like AI wrote it. Run every draft through this list before presenting.

### 1. "Not X, just Y" constructions
❌ "Not a career pivot — a network investment."  
❌ "Not about prestige. About who you become."  
✅ "People debate whether the MBA is worth it for the salary bump — that's the wrong question."

### 2. Em dash overuse
❌ "The insight — which most people miss — is that prestige isn't about the diploma."  
✅ "The insight most people miss is that prestige isn't about the diploma."

One or two em dashes in a full script is fine. Five is not.

### 3. Thought leadership opener language
❌ "In today's rapidly changing landscape..."  
❌ "Now more than ever..."  
❌ "The future of work is here."  
✅ Just say the thing directly.

### 4. Bullet-pointed advice ending
❌ "Three things to remember: one, document your expertise. Two, find your audience. Three, define your positioning."  
✅ "Start documenting your expertise now, stack what makes you unique, and talk about how you're using AI at work — that's the most undermonetized angle right now."  
(Note: this is still a list, but it flows as a sentence, not a PowerPoint)

### 5. Career-coach vocabulary
❌ "Define your unique positioning"  
❌ "Leverage your transferable skills"  
❌ "Build your personal brand strategy"  
❌ "Identify your target audience"  
❌ "Step into your authentic self"  
✅ Speak like a smart friend would — say "figure out who you're actually talking to" or "stack the things that make you weird"

### 6. Reporting the news
❌ "This article talks about how there's a growing segment..."  
❌ "Researchers found that..."  
❌ "According to a new study..."  
✅ Absorb the information and speak it in first person: "there's a growing segment of creators now earning over $100k — people like Cherie, a Stanford MBA and former PM, or me"

### 7. Generic motivational language
❌ "You have what it takes."  
❌ "Your dreams are valid."  
❌ "The world needs your unique gifts."  
✅ Specific and tactical, always.

### 8. "Future-proof" or similar tech buzzwords
❌ "Future-proof your career"  
❌ "Stay ahead of the curve"  
❌ "In the age of disruption"  
✅ Be direct about the actual thing happening.

### 9. Staccato choppiness
❌ "The MBA is misunderstood. It's not a career pivot. That ROI is dying. It's a $250k membership. Prestige matters here."  
✅ "Most people debate whether the MBA is worth it for the salary bump — but that's the wrong question. It's a $250k membership to one of the most curated networks of high achievers on earth, and prestige matters here not for the resume but because it filters who's in the room with you."

Short punchy lines are a tool, not a default. Use them for the payoff, not throughout.

### 10. Pivoting to Hannah's backstory unprompted
❌ "When I left banking to become a creator..."  
❌ "My journey from Goldman Sachs to content creator taught me..."  
✅ Use the credential to validate the claim, not as a narrative: "I worked at Morgan Stanley — those rooms operate exactly like this."

### 11. Opening with a question
❌ "Have you ever wondered why the most successful people still feel inadequate?"  
✅ State the insight directly or use a "if you are..." mirror hook.

### 12. Closing with "I hope this helps" or similar
❌ "I hope this gives you something to think about!"  
❌ "Let me know in the comments what you think!"  
✅ End with one clean line: a follow CTA, a punchy insight, or a save/share prompt.

## The Choppiness Problem in Detail

The most common failure mode. Choppy scripts read like a list of tweets — each sentence is self-contained and punchy, but there's no connective tissue. The ideas don't flow into each other. It sounds like bullets being read aloud.

**What causes it:**
- Writing each thought as its own sentence with a period
- Using em dashes to connect what should be one flowing thought
- Trying to make every line quotable rather than letting the argument build

**How to fix it:**
Connect ideas with: "and," "but," "because," "which means," "so," "which is why," "meanwhile," "and what that means for you is," "the reason this matters is"

**Test:** Read the script aloud at normal speaking pace. If it sounds like you're reading a list of bullet points, it's choppy. If it sounds like you're explaining something to a friend, it's flowing.

## Credentials Reference

Use these to anchor claims — deploy the right one for the right topic:

| Credential | Deploy for |
|---|---|
| Columbia undergrad | Ivy League / prestige / peer effect discussions |
| Morgan Stanley investment banking | Banking culture, prestige trap, corporate grind, financial skills |
| Wharton MBA | Network ROI, MBA debates, elite school value, business school credibility |
| Startup work (current, <100 people, 2-person marketing team, NYC) | Tiny teams, generalist roles, AI tools at work, startup vs. corporate |
| Nonlinear News newsletter | Creator economy, content monetization, building an audience |
| B2B brand deals / creator partnerships | Corporate creator economy, brand deals, professional niche value |
| Events / community building | In-person networking, community, high-achiever networks |

**Rule:** Use max 1-2 credentials per reel. The credential earns the right to make the claim — it isn't the story.
