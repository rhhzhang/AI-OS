# Script Examples

Real published scripts from Hannah's content calendar. Use these to calibrate tone, flow, structure, and length before writing anything new.

## Example 1: Research/News + Personal Reframe
**Title:** The Great BS Job Recession  
**Published:** March 2026  
**Format:** AI series walking reel

> The great bullshit job recession is here and there's never been a better time to become nonlinear.
>
> Welcome to this episode of the nonlinear playbook where I help you forge a path that AI can't replicate.
>
> Anthropic published its new labor market report last week that maps the gap between what AI can theoretically do in your job versus what it's actually doing right now. And it's saying that AI can replace 80-90% of tasks in areas like business, finance, legal, accounting.
>
> But the key thing a lot of people are missing by just reading the headline is that AI is replacing tasks, not jobs. Specifically bullsh*t tasks that are pushing paper around and ticking boxes, according to the anthropologist David Graeber who wrote this book.
>
> What the report is actually saying is if you're an analyst in finance or consulting who's always worked in a siloed role at a big corporate where everyone has a single defined scope, and you've been there for years — your tasks are the ones most replaceable by AI.
>
> It's already happening — McKinsey is down 25% from its peak headcount and now has 20,000 AI agents working alongside staff.
>
> But, if you have a nonlinear career, you've zig zagged across different functions, industries…AI can't replicate you bc it's only trained to follow predictable patterns.
>
> What used to be unconventional is becoming the new normal because the linear paths at big corporates are the ones disappearing.
>
> If you don't have a nonlinear path yet, that doesn't mean quit tomorrow and become a plumber — though you can. It means you can build a portfolio of experiences that makes you impossible to replicate by anyone else, or any AI for that matter. Pivot to a small startup where your role touches everything. Build skills across functions. Or yes, finally build something of your own.
>
> Stick around and I'll keep showing you how.

**What to notice:** The "but the key thing a lot of people are missing" move. She reads the headline, gives the non-obvious interpretation, then makes it personal (the analyst in finance). Never says "this report says" — she absorbs the info and speaks it. Ends with an action ladder (pivot / build skills / build something) then one CTA.

## Example 2: Concept + Story + Tactical Points
**Title:** Status Is A Tool, Not A Destination  
**Format:** Walking reel (sequel to 500k-view reel)  
**Status:** Filmed

> Status is the tool, not the destination if you want to build wealth.
>
> I made a video about why you should be low status but high income to build wealth and got over half a million views, but I'm afraid a lot of you got the idea that you shouldn't care about status if you want to be rich.
>
> Status is incredibly useful.
>
> I went to an Ivy League university for undergrad, that got me a six figure job out of college. I did Morgan Stanley investment banking, which still gets companies to look at my resume. And I have an MBA from Wharton, which means a lot of people take me more seriously, even on social media.
>
> Status gets you credibility, proximity, and opportunity. It opens doors, but the people who actually build wealth are the ones who walk through and use status as a tool to do something valuable on the other side that no longer relies on having status.
>
> So if you want to use status as a tool —
>
> One, use your background to command a premium in fields where it's rare. Use it to land the job, the first client, the intro.
>
> Two, use it to build your own signal — a personal brand is status you own, not status tied to an institution that can lay you off. No shame in using that institution to build it.
>
> Three, use it as a safety net. If the low-status move doesn't work, you can always go back. A former McKinsey consultant has a built-in reputational and financial cushion.
>
> Follow if you're using status as a tool to build a career on your own terms.

**What to notice:** Sequel hook directly references the prior viral video — earns instant credibility from the 500k views. Personal credentials listed as a rapid-fire block (Columbia, Morgan Stanley, Wharton) — 3 sentences, then the insight. Three numbered tactical points in plain English, not career-coach language. Clean follow CTA.

## Example 3: Three Real Examples
**Title:** $1B Solopreneur  
**Published:** April 2026  
**Format:** Walking reel

> The job market is so cooked, you might as well build a billion-dollar company. Here are 3 real companies you could have started in your 5-9.
>
> In 2024, Sam Altman bet his CEO group chat that a one-person billion-dollar company would exist within a year.
>
> The New York Times recently published a piece about Matthew Gallagher, who built a GLP-1 telehealth company called Medvi that's on track to do $1.8 billion in revenue this year. He started it with $20K, using Claude and ChatGPT for branding, marketing, customer service, and ads. They have 2 full time employees — him and his brother.
>
> Obsidian is a notes app with 9 employees doing $25M ARR with zero outside funding. They're like Notion but let you store everything locally so you own all your own data.
>
> Cal AI is an AI calorie counting app that two high schoolers built that estimates your calorie intake using a photo. They have 15 million downloads, $30 million ARR, seven employees, and got acquired by MyFitnessPal.
>
> None of these are technical AI companies but they used AI to build in other industries. And none of them had a groundbreaking idea. They just solved really specific, painful problems and had either timing, distribution, community, or a combo of all three on their side.

**What to notice:** Hook is a reframe — bad news (job market) becomes opportunity (build a company). Three examples are hyper-specific: company names, founder names, revenue numbers, employee counts, tools used. No vague "this startup did well." The ending insight is the pattern across the three: not genius ideas, but timing + distribution + community. No explicit CTA — the examples speak for themselves.

## Example 4: List Format
**Title:** Underrated Job Boards  
**Published:** April 2026  
**Format:** Walking reel

> If you're a smart ambitious person looking for a job, here are 5 places to find interesting roles that aren't LinkedIn.
>
> I love LinkedIn but some of the coolest jobs, especially at startups, never get posted there.
>
> **1 — Next Play** is a great list of weekly jobs from mostly early stage startups, but also people looking for co-founders. They also have a community and you can fill a form to get matched to new roles before they even make the newsletter.
>
> **2 — VC portfolio job boards.** Most big VCs post roles at their portfolio companies. a16z has a whole talent team that does this — Jordan Mazer posts a lot of jobs, you can also join their talent network and they'll intro you directly to founders. I'll put some other top VCs in the caption.
>
> **3 — Specialized recruiting firms like Omna Search and Golden Gate Recruits**, founded by ex McKinsey and Bain consultants.
>
> **4 — This one's more of a trick than a resource**, but a lot of startups post jobs as normal text posts, not formal job listings on LinkedIn and X because they don't want to pay to list. Search "hiring" plus the role you want to see these. This is also a great way to find out who the hiring manager is. I share a lot of informal job postings like this in my weekly newsletter.
>
> **5 — In person is back!** Search for events related to your industry on Luma, go to conferences like New York Tech Week, ask your coworkers, or be crazy like me and build a personal brand so you get invited to things.
>
> Save this and send it to a friend who's looking.

**What to notice:** Hook is a direct value promise — format (5 places), specificity (not LinkedIn), audience (smart ambitious person looking for a job). Each item has a name, a brief explanation, and ideally a specific detail (Jordan Mazer at a16z, the "hiring" search trick). "This one's more of a trick than a resource" — honest, conversational. Ends with save/send CTA.

## Example 5: Research/News + Borrowed Authority + Personal
**Title:** AI Series: Personal Brand  
**Published:** March 2026  
**Format:** AI series

> If you want to be the highest paid person in the age of AI, be a Rolex not an Omega.
>
> Paul Graham — the founder of Y Combinator — published his first essay in 9 months called The Brand Age, and it's about how brand is the only thing left when technology makes everything else a commodity, using a lesson from the Swiss watch industry.
>
> Before 1969, Swiss companies made the best watches in the world because they were the most accurate. Then quartz came along — cheaper, better, and suddenly anyone could make an accurate watch. Two Swiss watchmakers made two very different moves.
>
> Omega focused on improving their product and pricing — they mass-produced quartz watches and cut costs to try to outcompete cheaper watches from Japan. Rolex did the exact opposite. They barely touched quartz and invested in brand — craftsmanship, exclusivity, and advertising. They made Rolex scarce. Today, Rolex outsells Omega 3 to 1.
>
> AI is creating this exact moment for your career. If you want to be a Rolex, you need to stand out.
>
> It's not just about building any personal brand — but building one on your unique perspective and mix of skills, especially if you have a nonlinear path, that makes you so differentiated that you're irreplaceable.
>
> I did that after quitting banking to work across startups and the creator economy, and my personal brand has gotten me job offers and a business that pays more than my full time job.

**What to notice:** Strong borrowed authority pattern — Paul Graham introduced with specific credibility (YC founder), one insight extracted (brand vs. commodity), analogy extended to careers. The Swiss watch story builds narrative tension (Omega vs. Rolex). Personal story is compressed: one sentence after the full analogy is built. Ends with outcome (job offers + business > salary) not with advice.

## Example 6: Solution/Walking Reel
**Title:** Solution to the BS Job Recession  
**Published:** April 2026

> The solution to the bullshit job recession is meaningful work at startups with tiny teams, where this is the kind of person that will thrive.
>
> Anthropic published a report, which many of you have probably already read, about how AI can cover 90% of tasks across business, finance, management, and admin.
>
> A lot of this bullshit work happens at big companies, where you do 10 versions of a deck and spend most of your time chasing stakeholder alignment and approvals. That is why we are seeing layoffs at these bigger companies: those companies simply do not need as much admin and middle management anymore.
>
> Meanwhile, smaller companies are filling the gap. The NY Times just published a piece on the "tiny team" moment. A startup founder coined the "two-slice team," a spinoff of Jeff Bezos' two pizza rule, except now the argument is that only two slices are needed. The person eats the pizza. The AI agent doesn't get hungry.
>
> Ramp just posted a job listing that pretty much sums up the type of people startups are looking for: "Work best without permission. Default to 'how could I automate this.' Have more Claude agents than cousins." Cringe but true.
>
> I work at a startup with under 100 people. My marketing team is two people. We are not wasting our lives asking for a million approvals. We do interesting, meaningful work.
>
> This is great news if you have a varied, nonlinear set of experiences. On a smaller team, you cover more range, build across functions, and do work that is actually interesting. The high-agency generalist who knows how to build with AI is the person who will thrive in these smaller teams, or build their own.

**What to notice:** "Meanwhile" as a transition — does a lot of work. "Cringe but true" — that's her voice. The personal anchor (startup, 2-person marketing team) is one sentence and immediately useful. "This is great news if..." — speaks directly to the specific person who benefits. Ending is the insight about who thrives, not a list of advice steps.

## Key Patterns Across All Examples

1. **Ideas connect.** "And," "but," "which means," "meanwhile," "because" — the connective tissue is visible.

2. **Specific numbers and names are everywhere.** Not "a startup" but "Matthew Gallagher / Medvi / $1.8 billion / 2 employees." Real names earn trust.

3. **Personal anchor is brief.** Longest personal section is the status reel's 3 credentials — and that's the point of the reel, so it's justified. Usually 1-2 sentences.

4. **Endings land on one thing.** A follow CTA, a save/share prompt, a reframe that's the emotional payoff. Never a three-bullet summary.

5. **The news is a jumping off point, not the content.** "Anthropic published a report... but here's what most people are missing" — she absorbs the news and delivers the non-obvious interpretation.

6. **Voice is casual but the ideas are high quality.** "Cringe but true." "BC." "Rly." But paired with "The high-agency generalist who knows how to build with AI is the person who will thrive." The register is low; the content is high.
