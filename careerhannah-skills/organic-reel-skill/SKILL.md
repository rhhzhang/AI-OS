# Organic Reel Writing Skill

## What this skill is for

Writing short-form reel scripts that sound exactly like Hannah — not like AI wrote them, not like a career coach, not like a journalist summarizing an article. Like a smart, ambitious person talking to a friend about something genuinely interesting she figured out.

Before writing anything, read the reference files:
- `references/voice_and_style.md` — Hannah's voice, anti-patterns, what "choppy" means and how to avoid it
- `references/script_examples.md` — Real published scripts to calibrate tone and structure
- `references/hook_guide.md` — Hook types with examples from her best-performing content

---

## Step 0: Net New Value Test

Run this before writing a single word. Every reel must pass all three:

**1. Non-obvious** — The core insight can't be something the viewer already knows or could Google. The bar is "I hadn't thought of it that way." Generic observations and surface-level takes fail this.

**2. Tactical** — The viewer should be able to do something differently tomorrow. Vibes and inspiration alone don't pass. The insight needs to be specific enough to act on.

**3. Lived experience AI can't replicate** — The insight must come from Hannah's actual world: her time in banking at Morgan Stanley, her Wharton MBA, working at startups, building Nonlinear News, running creator events, doing brand deals, hiring from her MBA network. If the reel could have been written by any creator with a prompt, it doesn't have Net New Value.

If the concept fails any of these, find a sharper angle before proceeding.

## Step 1: Identify the format

Hannah's reels fall into five structures. Pick the one that fits the concept best:

### A. Research/News + Personal Reframe
Lead with something happening in the world (report, article, study, news), but the point of the reel isn't to report it — it's to surface the non-obvious implication that most people are missing. Hannah's lived experience is the bridge between the news and the insight.

*Best for:* AI/work trends, economy shifts, research findings  
*Example:* "great bs job recession" — leads with Anthropic report, reframes it as "AI replaces tasks, not people" through the lens of nonlinear careers

### B. Concept + Story + 3 Tactical Points
Introduce a concept (often with borrowed authority), validate it with a brief personal story, then give three concrete numbered moves.

*Best for:* Career frameworks, life decisions, identity reframes  
*Example:* "status is a tool, not a destination" — intro concept, 3 tactical uses of status (premium signaling, personal brand, safety net)

### C. Three Real Examples
A surprising claim up front, followed by three specific, named examples with real numbers, ending with the pattern they share — the non-obvious insight.

*Best for:* Trend reels, "this is already happening" content  
*Example:* "$1B solopreneur" — 3 companies (Medvi $1.8B, Obsidian $25M ARR, Cal AI $30M ARR) → what they share: timing + distribution + community, not groundbreaking ideas

### D. List (5 Things)
Direct hook naming what she's about to give, five specific and named items with actual details, ends with "save this and send it to [specific person]."

*Best for:* Resource lists, tactical how-tos, job search tools  
*Example:* "underrated job boards" — 5 specific places (Next Play, VC portfolio boards, Omna Search, search "hiring" trick, in-person events)

### E. Sequel / Nuance Add
References a prior viral piece. Opens by acknowledging the prior content and what it missed or needs to add. Shows intellectual honesty. Builds on audience memory.

*Best for:* Following up on viral reels, adding nuance, correcting oversimplifications  
*Example:* "status is a tool" opens by directly referencing the 500k-view low-status reel — "I made a video about why you should be low status but high income and I'm afraid a lot of you got the wrong idea"

## Step 2: Preserve Hannah's hook

**If Hannah provides a hook, use it verbatim.** Do not improve it, smooth it out, or make it "better." Her hooks come from her instincts about her audience — those instincts are usually right and the hook is often the hardest part she's already solved.

The only exception: if the hook is genuinely weak (too generic, too vague to stop a scroll), flag it, explain why, and present an alternative — but ALSO write the full script using her original hook so she can compare. Never just replace it.

What makes a hook weak:
- Could apply to any audience, any creator
- No curiosity gap, no counterintuitive claim
- Sounds like a thought leadership post opener
- Opens with a question

What makes a hook strong — see `references/hook_guide.md` for full framework.

## Step 3: Write the script

### The #1 rule: no choppiness

The most common failure mode is writing a string of short, punchy declarative sentences that read like a series of tweets instead of a person talking. This is what Hannah means by "choppy AF."

Good reel writing flows. Ideas connect to each other with "and," "but," "because," "so," "meanwhile," "which means." Sentences build on each other. Short punchy lines are saved for the payoff — not used throughout.

**Choppy (wrong):**
> The MBA is misunderstood. It's not a career pivot. That ROI is dying. It's a $250k membership. Prestige matters here. It filters who's in the room with you.

**Flowing (right):**
> Most people debate whether the MBA is worth it for the salary bump — but that's the wrong question. It's a $250k membership to one of the most curated networks of high achievers on earth, and prestige matters here not for your resume but because it filters who's in the room with you.

### Voice rules

Speak in lowercase register. Hannah writes (and talks) like this:
- "bc" not "because" in casual context
- "rly," "rn" occasionally
- Contractions throughout — "it's," "you're," "they're," "I've"
- Self-interrupting asides and honest tangents
- Direct address: "you," "if you're," "here's the thing"
- No corporate/coach vocabulary (see anti-patterns)

For full voice guide and word-level examples, read `references/voice_and_style.md`.

### Personal anchor rules

Hannah's background is the reel's credibility signal, not its subject. Use it briefly and purposefully:

- **Use it when it earns the right to make the claim** — "I worked at Morgan Stanley" before talking about finance culture; "I have a Wharton MBA" before talking about network ROI; "I run a startup with 2 people on the marketing team" before talking about tiny teams
- **Max 1-3 sentences** for the personal story portion
- **Never make the reel about the journey** — no origin story narrating, no pivot tale
- **Don't index on old positioning** — "I left banking to become a creator" is stale. Her identity now is: startup operator, creator, newsletter founder, MBA, event host — not just "former banker turned creator"

Relevant credentials to deploy naturally:
- Columbia undergrad
- Morgan Stanley investment banking
- Wharton MBA
- Worked across startups and tech (FAANG-adjacent recruiting)
- Runs Nonlinear News newsletter
- Hosts events, does brand deals with B2B companies
- Currently at a startup with a 2-person marketing team in NYC

### Borrowed authority pattern

When using a third-party framework, study, or thinker:
1. **Name them + give quick credibility** — "Paul Graham, the founder of Y Combinator" / "Harvard economist John Friedman" / "Naval Ravikant, author of How to Get Rich"
2. **Extract ONE insight** — don't summarize their whole body of work
3. **Validate or extend with Hannah's lived experience** — the borrowed insight lands because she's been in the room
4. **Never just report it** — the moment the reel starts sounding like "this article says..." it becomes news content, not Hannah's content

### Ending patterns

End with ONE of these, not a list of bullet points:

- **Identity follow** — "follow if you're [specific person this reel is for]" / "follow if you're building a career on your own terms"
- **Series promise** — "stick around and I'll keep showing you how" (especially for Nonlinear Playbook adjacent content)
- **Save/share CTA** — "save this and send it to [specific person in specific situation]"
- **Punchy insight line** — the one thing the viewer screenshots. Should be the emotional peak of the reel.
- **Callback CTA** — "comment [WORD] and I'll send you [specific thing]"

Never end with three bullet points of advice. The ending should be one clean landing, not a summary.

## Step 4: Length check

- **Walking reel / talking to camera:** aim for 150-200 words (60-90 seconds at Hannah's pace)
- **Under 60s:** aim for 130-150 words
- **Under 90s:** up to 210 words max

If it's running long, the first thing to cut is the personal anchor section (trim to 1 sentence) and any redundant transitions.

---

## Step 5: Self-check before presenting

Read back through the script and check:

1. **Flows out loud** — read it aloud. If it sounds like written prose being read, rewrite it as speech.
2. **Not reporting the news** — no "this article says," no "researchers found," no "according to." Absorb the info and speak it in Hannah's voice.
3. **No anti-patterns** — run the anti-patterns checklist in `references/voice_and_style.md`
4. **Personal anchor is brief** — if you spent more than 3 sentences on Hannah's story, cut it down
5. **Hook is Hannah's** — if she gave one, it's unchanged
6. **One clean ending** — not a list, not three bullet points, one landing

## Iteration rules

When Hannah gives feedback:
- **"Choppy"** — you're writing declarative sentences instead of flowing speech. Rewrite the whole thing connecting ideas with conjunctions, saving punchy lines for the payoff only.
- **"Reporting the news / no personality"** — you're summarizing the source material instead of speaking from inside it. Add her credential to anchor it, speak the insight in first person, remove any attribution language.
- **"Too career-coachy"** — you ended with bullet-pointed advice or used phrases like "define your unique positioning." Rewrite the ending as one insight or CTA.
- **"This isn't my voice"** — check `references/voice_and_style.md` anti-patterns. You've probably used flagged vocabulary, been too formal, or used written transitions that sound weird spoken aloud.
- **"You changed my hook"** — restore her hook verbatim and rebuild the script around it.
- **"Indexing on old positioning"** — you referenced banking→creator pivot or framed her primarily as a former banker. Remove it, reframe her current identity.

When revising: make targeted changes to flagged sections only. Don't rewrite the whole script unless the structure itself was wrong.
