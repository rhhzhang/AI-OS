# Interview Writing Skill

## Purpose
Help Hannah Zhang create interview briefs for newsletter guests and transform interview transcripts into polished Nonlinear News posts that match her authentic voice and editorial style.

## When to Use This Skill
- When Hannah is preparing to interview someone for her newsletter
- When Hannah has an interview transcript and needs to turn it into a published post
- When Hannah needs help structuring questions or identifying the narrative arc

---

## Part 1: Creating Interview Briefs

### Step 0: Research the Guest

Before writing a single question, research the guest thoroughly using web search and any links Hannah provides:
- LinkedIn (career path, timeline, roles, education)
- Their content (Instagram, Substack, YouTube, LinkedIn posts — whatever they're active on)
- Any interviews, podcasts, or articles they've been featured in
- Their website/business (what they actually do, who they serve, how they position themselves)
- Anything public they've said that's interesting, contrarian, or revealing

The goal is to understand this person well enough to know what questions will unlock something interesting vs. what will get a boring rehearsed answer. Don't just skim — actually read their stuff.

### Step 1: Confirm the Angle with Hannah

After research, present Hannah with:
- A summary of what you found (their path, what's interesting, gaps you noticed)
- 2-3 suggested angles based on how this person's story aligns with Hannah's audience and positioning
- What you think the narrative arc could be

Hannah picks the direction. Then you draft questions. Don't skip this step — the angle determines everything.

### The Producer Mindset
**Critical question before writing anything: Would you actually watch this interview?**

Think like a producer, not a template-filler:
- Does the narrative flow naturally?
- Are we highlighting what makes THIS person's story unique?
- Will these questions lead to insights the audience hasn't heard before?
- Are we asking questions that create a conversation, not an interrogation?

### Opening Note to Guest

Keep it short and casual. Three parts:

**1. One line of excitement** — genuine, brief, specific to this conversation. Mention that questions are prepped but the conversation will be casual.

**2. Audience + what they'd learn** — bolded audience description in one line, then bullet points of the specific topics you want to cover. This is for the guest's benefit so they know what to prepare for.

**3. Flexibility + logistics** — "If there's anything you'd like me to ask or highlight (or NOT), we'll work that in!" Plus logistics: this is a spoken conversation, will be recorded, newsletter draft sent for review before posting, published on Substack/Instagram/LinkedIn.

**Good example (Chelsea brief):**

*I'm so excited to share your story with my audience! I prepped a few questions, but we'll keep this fairly casual as that always makes for the best conversations. If there's anything you'd like me to ask or highlight (or NOT), we'll work that in!*

***My readers are ambitious people building nonlinear careers** — a lot of them are leaving finance, consulting, and tech, starting their own things, and figuring out who they are outside of a corporate identity. **I think they'd learn a ton from:***
- *how you built Good Lines from scratch*
- *what it's actually like to turn a passion into a lifestyle business*
- *your perspective on personal style when you're no longer handed a dress code*
- *your thoughts on taste and curation, because that's becoming one of the most valuable skills in the post-AI era*

***Logistics:** This will be a spoken conversation. I will record our call, create a newsletter draft for your review before posting (in a few weeks). When publishing, I share the newsletter on Substack, Instagram, and LinkedIn.*

**What NOT to do:**
- Write a long flattery paragraph about why they're interesting
- Over-explain Hannah's audience in prose form (use the bolded line + bullets format)
- Make it formal or stiff
- Skip the logistics (guests want to know the process)
- Tell the guest things they already know ("we met on Instagram" — they know that)

### Preface (for the published piece / video)

**What to include:**
- Who the guest is (background, current role, key credential)
- The narrative arc of their story
- What makes this conversation valuable
- Personal connection point (if relevant)

**Format:**
"In this conversation I sat down with [Name] — [what they do / their journey in one sentence].

[Personal connection — how Hannah knows them, why she wanted to have this conversation].

We talk about [3-4 key topics from the conversation]."

**Note:** Use "In this conversation I sat down with" not "Today I'm talking with" (too verbal for written format).

### Question Crafting Principles

**CRITICAL: Don't put words in their mouth**

BAD: "In March 2020, you recruited 60 volunteers and helped hundreds of people get jobs during the pandemic. That's a massive undertaking. What made you decide to do that?"

GOOD: "What happened in March 2020 that changed things for you?"

**Why?** Let them tell their story. Don't recount it for them.

**Think from first principles:**
1. What do I want to know?
2. What will lead them to share the insight naturally?
3. What creates conversation vs. recitation?

**Questions should feel like something you'd ask a friend over drinks.** Simple, direct, easy to answer, but designed to go somewhere interesting. The guest should be able to start talking immediately without needing to parse a complex question.

**Good question patterns:**
- "What were you doing before [X]? What was the first job you had out of college?" (simple, concrete, gets them talking)
- "How did you first get into [X]?" (open, lets them choose the starting point)
- "Was there a moment where you realized this could be a real business, or did it happen gradually?" (gives them two paths to choose from)
- "What's the biggest mistake people make when [X]?" (insight extraction without being formulaic)
- "How do you feel about [X]?" (simple, emotional, conversational)

**Weave in personal context naturally.** If Hannah has a personal connection to the topic, fold it into the question: "Your content is how I found you in the first place — it's a great mix of your own looks, client transformations, testimonials, process stuff. How did you figure out what to post?" This makes it feel like a real conversation, not a press junket.

**Merge related questions.** Don't have three separate questions when one with a slash works: "How do you protect the thing you love from becoming the thing that burns you out? / What would you say to someone who's worried that turning their passion into a business will ruin it?" This gives the guest flexibility in how they answer.

**Add casual real-life context inline.** "The dress code disappears overnight. Esp now with WFH." — dropping in a casual observation keeps it from feeling like an interview and more like a conversation.

**Avoid:**
- "Looking back, what throughline connects your experiences?" (too formulaic)
- "How would you describe your career so far?" (boring, generic)
- Vague references: "this whole process" — what process specifically?
- Questions that recount their story instead of letting them tell it
- Overly engineered multi-part questions that are hard to answer naturally
- Questions that sound like they came from a podcast template

### Fact-Checking is Essential

**Before writing questions, verify:**
- Timeline of their career moves (LinkedIn)
- Where they worked when (don't assume)
- Their primary platforms (is it YouTube? LinkedIn? Instagram?)
- Specific accomplishments they've shared publicly

**Example of getting it wrong:**
Assuming Yuqi moved to London during Goldman when she actually moved there for the startup role.

**How to avoid:**
Read their LinkedIn carefully. Don't rely on memory or assumptions.

### Structure: Sections Should Tell a Story

**Typical structure (4-6 sections):**
1. **Before / origins** — What were they doing before? How did they get here?
2. **Building** — How did they start? What did growth look like?
3. **The messy middle** — What was hard? What do people not see from the outside?
4. **Expertise / insights** — What do they know that their audience needs to hear?
5. **Advice** — For people in similar positions, what would they say?

This is flexible. Not every interview is a career pivot story. Some are about expertise, lifestyle businesses, or a specific worldview. Let the guest's story determine the sections, not a template.

**Within each section:**
- 3-6 questions that build on each other
- Mix of concrete (timeline, facts) and reflective (feelings, insights)
- Questions should flow conversationally — if you read them out loud in sequence, they should sound like a natural conversation
- Each section should have a clear narrative purpose

### Balance: Story + Insights

**For someone with an interesting journey:**
- 60% questions about their story/path
- 40% extracting insights/advice

**For someone who's an expert/coach:**
- 40% their story/how they got here
- 60% insights/advice they can offer

**For someone with a lifestyle business or creative career:**
- 40% how they built it
- 30% their expertise/craft
- 30% advice for people who want to do something similar

**Always include:**
- How they got to where they are
- Advice for people in similar positions
- Where people can find them

### Questions to Always Avoid

- "How would you describe your career/journey so far?" (lazy opening)
- "What's the throughline connecting your experiences?" (formulaic)
- Questions with the answer embedded in them
- Questions that tell their story instead of asking them to tell it
- Vague pronouns: "this process" "that experience" — be specific
- Generic questions that could work for anyone
- Overly long questions that are hard to parse in conversation

### Platform-Specific Considerations

**Don't assume the platform — check what they actually use:**
- If someone went viral on YouTube, focus on YouTube (not LinkedIn)
- If they're known for Instagram, don't over-emphasize their LinkedIn
- Ask about the platform where they actually have traction
- Understand what makes each platform different for them

**Example:**
Yuqi has 10K YouTube followers and viral videos — that's more interesting than her LinkedIn posts about mapping cities

### The "Would You Watch This?" Test

Before finalizing questions, ask:
- Does this flow like a conversation?
- Are these questions leading to insights or just facts?
- Am I highlighting what makes THIS person unique?
- Would I want to hear the answers to these questions?
- Am I avoiding formulaic patterns?
- Could a friend ask these questions at a dinner party and it would feel natural?

## Part 2: Transforming Transcripts into Newsletter Posts

### Pre-Writing Process
1. **Identify the narrative arc**: What's the actual story here? (Not just a career timeline)
2. **Find the thesis**: What's the one thing you want readers to take away?
3. **Spot the gold**: Which quotes are surprising, specific, or counterintuitive?
4. **Note what's missing**: What context does the reader need that isn't in the transcript?

### Structure
1. **Opening** (2-4 paragraphs)
   - Hannah's meta-commentary or personal context
   - Brief intro to the guest (credentials, what makes them interesting)
   - Why this conversation matters now

2. **Thematic sections** (## or ### headers)
   - Use descriptive headers that hint at insight: "Why Ed Stayed at Microsoft" not "Microsoft Career"
   - Each section should have a clear narrative purpose
   - Sections flow chronologically OR thematically (not always strict timeline)

3. **Q&A format**
   - **Hannah:** Question in bold
   - **Guest:** Answer as natural prose (not verbatim transcript)
   - Questions can be paraphrased/tightened from original brief
   - Answers should feel conversational but more polished than spoken word

4. **Closing**
   - Connection info (LinkedIn, Instagram, website, etc.)
   - Optional: Brief reminder of key takeaway or next step for readers

### Editing the Q&A
- **Keep it conversational**: "Yeah." "It really was." "Not at all."
- **Preserve personality**: Distinctive phrases, humor, metaphors
- **Cut the filler**: Remove verbal tics, tangents, redundancy
- **Add clarity**: Context that was implied in conversation but needs to be explicit in writing
- **Show, don't tell**: Keep specific examples, cut generic statements
- **Paragraph breaks**: Break up long answers into digestible chunks
- **Maintain rhythm**: Vary length of Q&A exchanges (some quick, some deep)

### Voice Consistency Checklist
- [ ] Does the opening feel like Hannah, not a generic newsletter?
- [ ] Are there specific details (numbers, names, moments) vs. abstractions?
- [ ] Does it challenge a common assumption or reveal something non-obvious?
- [ ] Are headers descriptive and intriguing (not just labels)?
- [ ] Does the Q&A feel like a real conversation, not a press release?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment where the guest is vulnerable or honest about struggle?
- [ ] Does it end with something actionable, reflective, or thought-provoking?

## Hannah's Newsletter Voice & Editorial POV

### Core Characteristics
- **Direct and conversational**: Writes like she's talking to a friend over coffee
- **Personal and meta**: Often opens with her own reflections
- **Focused on the messy middle**: Less interested in polished success stories
- **Anti-generic career advice**: Pushes back on template thinking
- **Specific over abstract**: Values concrete details, actual numbers
- **Warm but not saccharine**: Genuinely enthusiastic without being overly effusive

### What She Avoids
- Corporate jargon and buzzwords
- Excessive formatting (minimal bullets, headers only when necessary)
- Generic "5 tips for success" frameworks
- Putting people on pedestals
- Making assumptions about what's "right" for everyone
- Overly polished narratives that skip the struggle

### Audience
Smart, ambitious professionals (often early-to-mid career) who are:
- Questioning default paths (IB to PE, consulting to startup, etc.)
- Building careers that don't fit templates
- Seeking agency and authenticity over optimization
- Interested in personal brands, nonlinear careers, and intentional choices

## Key Reminders

- Hannah's newsletter is about **process, not prescription**
- The best posts reveal something **non-obvious** about career decisions
- **Specificity creates credibility** (numbers, names, exact moments)
- The goal is to help readers **think differently**, not give them a template
- Every conversation should challenge at least one common assumption
- Hannah's voice is **warm but not performative** — she's a real person sharing real conversations
- **Think like a producer**: Would you watch this? Does it flow?
- **Don't put words in the guest's mouth**: Let them tell their story
- **Check your facts**: Verify timeline, platforms, key details
- **Think from first principles**: What actually matters for THIS conversation?
- **Research first, angle second, questions third**: Never skip the research or angle confirmation steps
