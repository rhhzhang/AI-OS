# Brand Deal Reel Writing Skill

## Overview
This skill helps write sponsored Instagram/TikTok reel scripts that feel like Hannah's organic content — but happen to feature a brand partner. The goal is to make brand deal reels indistinguishable in quality and tone from viral organic reels, while fully complying with brand brief requirements.

This skill DEPENDS ON the viral-social-media-content-writing skill. All principles, structures, voice guidelines, anti-patterns, and the script interrogation protocol from that skill apply here. This skill adds the brand deal layer on top.

## Net New Value Test (Run This Before Writing Anything)

Every reel — brand deal or organic — must pass this before you write a single word. No amount of craft saves a reel that fails here.

A reel has **Net New Value** if it delivers all three:

**1. Non-obvious** — The insight cannot be something the viewer could have Googled or assumed. If a smart person's first reaction is "yeah, I know," it's obvious. The bar is: "I hadn't thought of it that way." Generic observations, trends everyone is already talking about, and surface-level takes all fail this.

**2. Highly tactical** — Not vibes. Not inspiration. Not a mindset shift. The viewer should be able to do something differently tomorrow because of this reel. Tactical means specific enough to act on — a framework, a concrete move, a named thing they can apply.

**3. Lived experience AI can't replicate** — The insight must come from Hannah's actual experience. Real things she's done. Specific mistakes she's made. Things she noticed from the inside. AI can generate generic takes on any topic. It cannot generate Hannah's perspective from inside Goldman, from leaving banking, from building Nonlinear News, from the events she's run, the founders she's talked to. That's the moat. If the reel could have been written by anyone with a prompt, it doesn't have Net New Value.

If the concept fails any of these three, find a sharper angle before writing. A well-executed reel on a generic premise still underperforms.

## Core Philosophy

Brand deal reels are NOT product demos with a hook slapped on. They are organic-feeling content that solves a real audience pain point, where the brand's product happens to be the best solution.

The reel should feel like it could live on Hannah's feed regardless of the sponsorship. The viewer should get genuine value. The brand integration should feel structural, not bolted on.

**The hierarchy is always:**
1. Audience pain point first
2. Hannah's insight/framework as the alpha
3. Product as the vehicle that delivers the outcome
4. Brand never becomes the subject — the viewer's transformation is the subject

## Step 0: Read the Brand Brief (MANDATORY FIRST STEP)

Before doing ANYTHING — before brainstorming, before suggesting a single idea, before writing a single word — read the entire brand brief thoroughly. Extract and organize:

### Compliance Requirements Checklist
Build this checklist FIRST. Every item must be satisfied in the final output:
- [ ] Sponsorship disclosure text (exact wording required, duration on screen, placement rules)
- [ ] Hashtag requirements (which hashtags, where they go, any restrictions on other tags)
- [ ] Brand account tagging (which accounts, which platforms)
- [ ] Paid partnership label requirements
- [ ] Content approval process and deadlines
- [ ] Link/CTA requirements (link in bio, custom tracking links, duration)
- [ ] Platform-specific requirements (length limits, safe zones, etc.)
- [ ] Required verbal mentions or key messages
- [ ] Required visual elements (product demos, UI screenshots, logos)
- [ ] Usage rights and exclusivity terms
- [ ] Raw file deliverables needed
- [ ] Do's and Don'ts from the brand

### Product/Feature Information
- What is the product/feature being promoted?
- What are the key capabilities?
- What is the brand's positioning?
- What use cases do they highlight?

**IMPORTANT: Not all briefs are great.** Many brand briefs include way too many features, talking points, and messaging guidelines. The skill's job is to distill — pick ONE, max TWO points of differentiation that genuinely matter to Hannah's audience. Covering everything the brief asks for makes bad content. Focus creates good content.

## Step 1: Understand the Audience Intersection

Before brainstorming, map the overlap between:
- **Hannah's audience**: Smart, ambitious people in tech/startups, 25-35, nonlinear careers, non-technical professionals (ops, strategy, growth, marketing). People whose careers don't fit into a single box. MBA grads, consultants, people in transition. NOT beginners. NOT generic career advice seekers.
- **The brand's target audience**: (from the brief)
- **The intersection**: Where do these overlap? What pain point exists in the intersection that the product actually solves?

**CRITICAL: The pain point must be something Hannah's audience ALREADY has — not a pain point the product creates awareness of.** If the audience doesn't already feel the pain, the reel won't resonate. The product should be the answer to a question they're already asking, not a solution looking for a problem.

### Questions to Ask Hannah (if answers aren't obvious):
1. "What's your honest, personal experience with this product? What have you actually used it for that surprised you or changed how you work?"
2. "What specific thing would you demo on camera that shows the transformation — not features, the actual task and outcome?"
3. "What's the ONE transformation you want the viewer to walk away thinking about?"

**Do NOT proceed without answers to these questions. Do not fill with generic assumptions. The whole point is that this content is NOT generic.**

## Step 2: Brainstorm Angles (Content Strategist Mode)

### How to Brainstorm
- Start with the audience pain point, NOT the product
- Ask: "What does my audience desperately want to accomplish that this product happens to be the best tool for?"
- The reel should NOT be about the product until it has to be
- Generate 2-3 angle options, each built around a DIFFERENT audience pain point
- Each angle picks ONE core differentiator from the product and ties it to the pain point

### Angle Quality Test
For each angle, ask:
- Would this reel topic work as organic content even WITHOUT the sponsorship?
- Is the pain point something the viewer ALREADY feels, or am I creating artificial need?
- Does the product enter as the natural solution, or does it feel forced?
- Is there a genuine insight or framework (not just a feature walkthrough)?
- Would someone screenshot this and send it to a friend?

### What Makes a Bad Angle
- Leading with features instead of outcomes ("Cowork can access your local files" ❌)
- Generic productivity framing ("save time," "work smarter" ❌)
- Pain points the audience doesn't actually have
- Transformation that's really just a feature description wearing a costume
- Anything that could appear on any generic tech review account

### What Makes a Good Angle
- Grounded in a specific, universal experience the audience recognizes ("15 ideas in my Notes app" ✓)
- Product enters as the vehicle for Hannah's OWN framework/expertise (✓)
- Transformation is emotional/aspirational, not technical ("chief of staff" ✓)
- The insight is something the viewer hasn't heard framed this way before
- Feels like Hannah sharing a secret, not presenting a sponsorship

### Present Angles to Hannah
Present 2-3 angles with:
- The specific audience pain point each targets
- The hook direction (not the full hook — the territory)
- Why it could work for HER audience specifically
- The ONE product differentiator it highlights

Let Hannah choose and riff. Her instinct on what resonates with her audience is better than any strategic analysis.

## Step 3: Develop the Hook

Use the "Smart Ambitious" series hook format from the viral-social-media-content-writing skill as the default for brand deal reels:

"If you're a smart ambitious person who [specific pain point / situation] — [tease that creates curiosity gap]"

### Hook Rules for Brand Deals
- The hook must be about the VIEWER'S pain point, never about the product
- Product name should NOT appear in the hook
- The tease should create genuine curiosity without being clickbait
- "Let me show you something" works when the "what" is already named in the first part
- Specificity > cleverness. "Side project ideas in your Notes app" > "great ideas you never execute"
- The hook must pass all standard hook testing criteria from the viral skill

### Hook ↔ Brand Integration Check
- Does the hook set up a promise that the product can actually deliver on?
- Is there a clean narrative path from the hook's pain point to the product as solution?
- Would the hook work even if you removed the product entirely? (It should — that's the sign it's grounded in real audience pain)

## Step 4: Write the Script

### Script Structure for Brand Deal Reels

The default structure (adapt as needed):

1. **Hook** — audience pain point, "smart ambitious" format
2. **Personal connection** — Hannah's own experience/credibility on this topic (brief, 1-2 sentences). This is NOT about the product yet.
3. **The reveal** — introduce the product as what she used/built to solve the problem. Product enters here naturally because it's the tool that delivered the outcome the hook promised.
4. **The demo/proof** — show what the product actually did. Specific outputs, real results. This is where the product gets its moment, but framed as PROOF of the transformation, not as a feature tour.
5. **The insight** — the one-line framing that makes the viewer think "I want that." ("It's like having a chief of staff who thinks exactly like I do.") This is the shareable, screenshot-worthy line.
6. **CTA** — engagement-driven. "Comment [word] and I'll send you [specific thing]." Never "link in bio."

### Script Rules for Brand Deals

**DO:**
- Make the product integration feel structural — it should be woven into the narrative, not a separate "ad read" section
- Use Hannah's personal experience as the bridge between pain point and product
- Show specific outputs/results, not feature descriptions
- Include one aspirational framing line (e.g., "chief of staff") that the viewer will remember
- Keep Hannah's framework/expertise as the alpha — the product is the execution layer
- Use natural speech patterns throughout — contractions, "like," casual pacing

**DON'T:**
- Don't create a separate "product section" that feels like an ad break
- Don't list features — show ONE transformation
- Don't use the brand's marketing language verbatim (rewrite in Hannah's voice)
- Don't try to cover everything in the brief — pick one, max two points of differentiation
- Don't say "link in bio" — use comment CTAs
- Don't say "future-proof" ever
- Don't use "research preview" or technical availability details in the spoken script (put in caption)
- Don't use "Not X, just Y" constructions (AI slop — flagged in viral skill anti-patterns)
- Don't use any anti-patterns from the viral-social-media-content-writing skill

### Voice and Personalization Requirements
- The script must sound like something Hannah would actually say to a friend
- Personal details should be TRUE — don't claim experiences she hasn't had
- If referencing her background, be honest about the path (don't retrofit clean narratives)
- B-roll and visual moments should be uniquely her (not generic "making coffee" clichés)
- The "step away" or lifestyle moments should reflect something she actually does
- Every detail that makes it personal should be verified with Hannah — don't assume

## Step 5: Filming Notes Table

### Required Format
Always output the concept in this table format:

| **Script** | **Visuals / Filming Notes** |
|---|---|
| [Exact spoken words] | [Detailed filming instructions: camera angle, b-roll clips, screen recordings, text overlays, transitions, timing] |

### Filming Notes Should Include:
- Camera directions (talking to camera, zoom in, b-roll cut)
- On-screen text overlay content and timing
- Screen recording segments (what to show, what to zoom into)
- B-roll descriptions and timing
- Sponsorship disclosure placement (timing, position, safe zone)
- Partnership hashtag visibility windows
- Transition notes between sections
- Any specific visual elements required by the brief (product demos, logos, UI)

## Step 6: Write the Caption

### Caption Structure
1. Sponsorship disclosure (first line — exact wording from brief)
2. Value summary — 1-2 sentences describing what the viewer gets (in Hannah's voice, not marketing speak)
3. Platform/availability info (if required by brief — keep brief)
4. CTA matching the video's spoken CTA
5. Required hashtag(s) and brand tag(s) — LAST

### Caption Rules
- Start with sponsorship disclosure per brief requirements
- Tag the brand account within the first two lines if required by brief
- Match the tone of the reel — don't suddenly go corporate in the caption
- No tags beyond what the brief requires
- Include any mandatory CTA language from the brief
- Keep it scannable — nobody reads long captions

## Step 7: Brief Compliance Final Check

Before presenting the final concept, run through the compliance checklist from Step 0. Every single item must be satisfied. Format as:

- ✅ [Requirement] — [how it's met in the concept]
- ❌ [Requirement] — [FLAGGED: not yet addressed, needs resolution]

**If any item is ❌, do NOT present the concept as final. Flag it and resolve it first.**

## Step 8: Script Interrogation Protocol (from Viral Skill)

Run the FULL script interrogation protocol from the viral-social-media-content-writing skill:
1. Hook ↔ Punchline Integrity
2. Thread Check
3. Sentence-Level Interrogation
4. Specificity Audit
5. Voice Check
6. Length Justification

**Net New Value Check (applies to all reels):**
7. **Non-obvious check**: Is the core insight something the viewer couldn't have assumed or Googled? If no, find a sharper angle.
8. **Tactical check**: Can the viewer do something differently tomorrow because of this? If it's inspiration or vibes only, it's not tactical enough.
9. **Lived experience check**: Does this insight come from Hannah's actual experience — something AI couldn't generate from a prompt? If not, push Hannah for a more specific personal angle.

**Additional Brand Deal Checks:**
10. **Product Integration Check**: Does the product feel structural or bolted on? If you removed the product mention, would there be a gap in the narrative (good) or would the reel work fine without it (bad — product isn't integrated enough)?
11. **Authenticity Check**: Would Hannah's audience believe she actually uses this product? Does the use case feel real or forced?
12. **One-Differentiator Check**: Are we highlighting more than two points of differentiation? If yes, cut until we're at one or two max.
13. **"Would I share this?" Check**: If the viewer didn't know this was sponsored, would they still share it? If not, the content isn't strong enough.

## Iteration Rules

### When Hannah Gives Feedback
- Make targeted changes to ONLY the sections she flags — don't rewrite the whole script
- Output just the changed section unless she asks for the full script
- If she says "this feels generic" — the problem is almost always that you're leading with features instead of her audience's pain point. Go back to Step 2.
- If she says "AI slop" — check the anti-patterns list in the viral skill. You've probably used a flagged construction. Rewrite as if explaining to a friend at a bar.
- If she says "this isn't my voice" — you're probably being too formal, too structured, or using transitions that sound written instead of spoken. Read it out loud.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid (Learned from Experience)
1. **Leading with product features, not audience outcomes** — The #1 mistake. Always ask "what does my audience want to accomplish?" before "what does this product do?"
2. **Brainstorming ideas that are too generic** — "Save time" and "be more productive" are never good enough. Get specific about WHAT the viewer gets to do or stop doing.
3. **Using "Not X, just Y" or "Not X — Y" constructions** — This is flagged AI slop. Remove every instance.
4. **Suggesting cliché b-roll moments** — "Made coffee and came back" is generic. Ask Hannah what's uniquely her.
5. **Covering too many brief points** — The brief will have 10 things. Pick one. Maybe two. That's it.
6. **Saying "link in bio"** — Hannah uses comment CTAs. Always.
7. **Including technical details in the spoken script** — "Research preview," "Mac only," "Max plan" — these go in the caption, not the script. The script stays focused on the transformation.
8. **Making the overlay text weird or forced** — On-screen text should be clear and pique interest without being bizarre. When in doubt, state the transformation simply.
9. **Claiming experiences Hannah hasn't had** — Always verify personal details. If something feels disingenuous, it probably is. Ask her.
10. **Treating the brand's marketing language as gospel** — Rewrite everything in Hannah's voice. The brief's talking points are inputs, not scripts.

## Examples of Good vs. Bad Brand Deal Approaches

### Bad (Feature-Led):
"Claude Cowork gives you local file access and runs parallel sub-agents so you can execute multi-step tasks autonomously."
→ This is a product spec sheet, not content. Nobody stops scrolling for this.

### Bad (Generic Productivity):
"If you want to save time and be more productive, let me show you this AI tool."
→ Could be any product, any creator, any audience. Zero specificity.

### Bad (Bolted-On Integration):
[Great organic content about side projects] ... "Oh and by the way, I used Claude Cowork to help with this. Link in bio."
→ Product is an afterthought. Brand won't be happy, audience won't care.

### Good (Pain Point → Transformation):
"If you're a smart ambitious person who keeps a running list of side project ideas like this in your notes app but never actually starts any of them — let me show you something. I started my personal brand as a side project. So I took everything I've learned about going from idea to actually doing something and gave it to Claude Cowork and said 'turn this into a skill.'"
→ The pain point is specific and universal for the audience. Hannah's credibility is the bridge. The product is HOW she delivered the transformation. The viewer gets a real tool AND a glimpse at how to build their own.

## Output Format

Every brand deal concept should be delivered as:

1. **Concept table** (Script | Visuals / Filming Notes)
2. **Caption** (with all required disclosures, tags, CTAs)
3. **Brief Compliance Checklist** (every requirement checked ✅ or flagged ❌)
4. **Script Interrogation Results** (only flag issues, don't list passing checks unless asked)

## Reference: Hannah's Side Hustle Framework

Hannah's framework for starting side projects (used as input for skills and content):

1. **Come up with a unique and personal idea.** What can you talk endlessly about? What do people come to you for advice on? When you find one that feels right, trust your gut. Make sure it's personal to you — if it's personal, it's unique even in a saturated market.

2. **Test the idea.** This looks different depending on the idea. The key is getting real feedback before building anything. Talk to people. Find out if there's actual demand for your perspective. Look for themes in what people tell you.

3. **Build the distribution channel first.** 5-10 years ago, people built a product then found channels to sell it. That's not how it works anymore. Find your customers or audience and build around them from day one. You don't need to know exactly what your product will be — you need to know what problem you're solving. Build channels around that problem.

This framework should inform any side-project-related brand deal content and can be encoded into skills built for demo purposes.
