# Carousel Skill

Transform Hannah's rough ideas into scroll-stopping carousel posts—visual, punchy, zero AI slop.

> **Platform note (2025-2026):** Carousels are performing on par with reels right now — not a backup format. Treat them as a primary content type, not a fallback.

## Step 0: Identify the Carousel Type

Every carousel falls into one of three buckets. Identify it before writing — it shapes the structure, tone, and goal.

**Personal** — Micro lifestyle vlog. Storytimes or lifestyle resets in 5-10 slides. Feels like a story, not a letter — people stay longer because they're flipping through a narrative, not reading a wall of text. Goal: connection and relatability.

**Valuable** — Hyper-saveable and shareable information rooted in Hannah's personal experience. Tips and breakdowns carry more weight when they come from something she's actually done — not generic advice. Dense, practical, screenshot-worthy. Goal: saves and shares that drive reach.

**Signature** — Take whatever series Hannah is known for and put it in carousel form (e.g., Nonlinear Icons). Can rotate with reels to keep the series format alive across content types. Goal: build recognizable format and series loyalty.

The type affects what hook structure to use, how dense the slides should be, and what the last slide asks for.

## Target Audience: Alex

Every carousel speaks to this persona:

**Who Alex is**: High-achieving person in their late 20s to mid-30s (sweet spot: 28-32). Elite credentials (Ivy+/top MBA or equivalent) or scrappy equivalent with an impressive trajectory. Currently working in finance, consulting, Big Tech, or startups — AND building something alongside it. Total comp $100K-$300K plus growing side income they're learning how to scale. NYC, SF, Chicago energy.

**The core tension**: Too ambitious and multi-dimensional for one lane. Wants to build wealth, influence, and freedom across multiple lanes — day job AND brand AND income streams — without any of them competing with the others.

**Alex's three stages (know which one you're writing for):**
- **Stage 1 — The Itch**: Still primarily in the 9-5, starting to build, asking "is this actually possible for someone like me?"
- **Stage 2 — The Build** (primary): Has both — figuring out how to make the 9-5 and the side build compound instead of compete. This is the main audience for most content.
- **Stage 3 — The Flywheel**: Side income is real ($30K-$150K+ outside the day job), now optimizing and thinking about the transition.

**What resonates**: Real numbers and real timelines. Positioning and narrative frameworks. Stories from people building both the career AND the side project simultaneously. Tactical content they can apply immediately. Builder proof, not escape inspiration.

**What doesn't land**: Escape-focused content, vague "follow your passion" advice, golden handcuffs framing as the primary hook, anything preachy or coaching-speak. Alex is already in motion — they don't need permission, they need frameworks.

Write like you're talking to Alex over coffee. They're smart, discerning, ambitious without being one-dimensional, and have zero patience for generic career content.

## Workflow

### 1. Understand Hannah's Direction

Hannah provides the concept—your job is to execute in her voice.

**She might give you**: Bullet points, voice memo transcripts, draft headlines, general direction ("carousel about building in public," "AI era polymath stuff," "the dual identity thing"), specific angles, half-formed thoughts.

**Your job**: Identify the hook angle (what stops Alex scrolling?), extract key concepts, preserve her specific phrases, fill gaps with details from her world (finance, tech, startups, MBA, NYC/SF/Chicago life, building on the side), structure for visual format.

---

### 2. Nail the Hook (Slide 1)

The cover is the only thing that determines whether anyone swipes. If slide one doesn't stop the scroll, the rest doesn't matter.

**Proven hook structures (pick the one that fits the content):**

- **Concept/Framework**: "The art of [concept] / the secret to [tips]" — elevated concept name + emotional outcome
- **Myth-busting**: "Stop [doing common action] until you know this" — pattern interrupt + curiosity gap
- **Process/Tutorial**: "How I [used specific tool] to [get specific result] in [specific timeframe]" — personal proof + specificity + time constraint
- **Belief disruptor**: "The worst advice on [topic] right now" — challenges something the audience already believes

**Good hooks (Hannah's style):**
- Direct with specific audience: "6 things high-achieving people are doing to own their careers in 2026"
- Lead with exclusive access: "LinkedIn invited me to a private creator event"
- Start with specific data: "Founder titles are up 70% on LinkedIn"
- Blunt statement with context: "Recruiters are posting fake job listings"

**Bad hooks (instant delete):**
- Vague clickbait: "What LinkedIn won't tell you about 2026"
- Generic: "Career advice you need to hear"
- Wrong audience: "6 things job seekers need to know" (too broad)
- Trying too hard: "The brutal truth about your career"
- Rhetorical questions: "Want to know the secret to..."

**Cover design notes** (include in visual note): micro-topic focus, negative hooks or numbers work best, bold easy-to-read fonts, consistent branding, tag Hannah's profile, follow CTA in top left.

---

### 3. Choose a Structure

Pick the framework that best fits the content and carousel type. Don't mix and match randomly — commit to one and follow through.

**The Story Arc (5-7 slides, simpler):**
- Slide 1: Hook
- Slide 2: Introduce the problem
- Slide 3: Offer the solution
- Slide 4: Deliver the value
- Slide 5: CTA

**The Comfort Framework (7 slides, deeper — best for valuable carousels):**
- Slide 1: Hook — disrupts a belief or shows how Hannah did something
- Slide 2: Context, results, or story behind the insight — add receipts/screenshots when possible
- Slide 3: Core message — the main lightbulb moment
- Slide 4: Flip the common belief the audience holds
- Slide 5: Break the idea into a digestible framework (this is what makes it saveable)
- Slide 6: Real-life examples (this is what turns saves into follows)
- Slide 7: Takeaway, reminder, or CTA

The transformation arc underneath both: Problem → Cost → Shift → System → Proof → Vision → Decision. Strategic sequencing is what keeps people reading to the end.

**The 2nd slide specifically** needs its own attention: use a different hook to re-pique curiosity, add a visual (meme, gif, image), prompt saves and swipes directly, highlight the problem or goal.

---

### 4. Structure Each Slide

Every slide needs clear visual hierarchy:

```
[HEADLINE]
Big, bold statement (5-8 words max)

[BODY COPY]
Supporting detail or context
2-3 short sentences max
Specific examples preferred
```

**Writing rules for every slide:**
- Write at a 6th grade reading level. Simpler language = wider reach = higher saves. Complexity excludes.
- One idea per slide. 5-7 lines maximum. Mobile readers bounce with dense text.
- Every slide must be scannable in 3 seconds.
- Every slide must be saveable on its own — standalone value, not dependent on context from the previous slide.
- Ask before including any slide: "Why should they care?" If you can't answer that clearly, cut it.

**Good slide:**
```
I worked 100 hours and learned nothing

The prestige wasn't worth it. I was building the 47th version of a slide deck at 3am while missing my friend's wedding. Banking taught me Excel shortcuts, not how to think.
```

**Bad slide (AI slop):**
```
The Hidden Cost of Prestige

While climbing the corporate ladder, I discovered that success isn't just about hours worked—it's about meaningful impact. This realization transformed my approach to career decisions.
```

---

### 5. Build Middle Slides

Each middle slide should:
- Make ONE point clearly
- Use specific examples (real numbers, brand names, situations)
- Stay in Hannah's voice
- Build on the previous slide but stand alone
- Stay under 50 words (preferably 30-40)
- Mix content types where possible (text, example, mini-framework, screenshot) to keep engagement up

**Patterns that work**: Before/after contrast, specific story or moment, concrete lesson, real example from her experience, numbered point with specific detail.

**Patterns to avoid**: Forced three-part lists, generic advice ("focus on what matters"), vague principles ("align with your values"), everything being "symbolic."

---

### 6. Close Strong (Last Slide)

The last slide matters as much as the first — it's where saves turn into follows. Give it real attention.

Include:
- Clear CTA (save this, follow for more, try this today, share with someone who needs it)
- Tag Hannah so non-followers can click through to her profile
- Keep it benefit-driven: what does the reader get from following?

**Good closing:**
```
The real optionality?

Committing to something. Picking a path. Getting so good at one thing that opportunities find you.

Stop optimizing. Start building.
```

**Bad closing:**
```
Ready to Transform Your Career?

Here's what to do next:
✨ Define your values
✨ Take aligned action
✨ Build your personal brand

Tag someone who needs this! 👇
```

## Self-Critique Checklist

Before delivering:

1. **Would Hannah actually post this?** Slide 1 stops scroll without being cringe? Each slide sounds like her talking, not a brand?

2. **Does it speak to Alex?** Would a 28-32 year old with elite credentials who's building something alongside their 9-5 relate? Addresses where they actually are (builder energy, not escape energy)? Uses their terminology?

3. **Free of AI carousel clichés?** No "Here's what nobody tells you...", no emoji explosion, no transition questions ("The catch?"), no inspirational corporate conclusions, no vague clickbait hooks.

4. **Works visually?** Each slide scannable at a glance? Under 50 words per slide? Clear headline + body pattern?

5. **Scroll-stopping?** Would YOU swipe through all slides?

6. **Saveable?** Does slide 5 (or equivalent) contain a digestible framework someone would screenshot?

7. **Last slide earns a follow?** Clear CTA, benefit-driven, no cringe?

## Nonlinear Icons Carousels

A recurring series profiling famous people with nonlinear career paths. The goal is to tell a cohesive story that highlights reinvention using real facts and research—not commentary or a laundry list of dates and events.

### Research First

Before writing, use web search to gather real details:
- Specific names (business partners, publishers, bosses)
- Real numbers (dollar amounts, dates, statistics)
- Actual places (company names, cities, specific locations)
- Verified quotes
- Timeline of key career pivots

Fact check claims before including them. If something can't be verified, cut it.

### Story Structure

**Slide 1: Hook**
- Series title: "NONLINEAR career paths" or "NONLINEAR ICONS"
- Career trajectory as subtitle: "wall street → catering → media business → America's first self-made female billionaire"
- Person's name
- Optional intriguing detail in parentheses

**Slides 2-7: The Story**
Tell the career story as a narrative, not a timeline. Each slide covers an era or pivot:
- Early career and how they got started
- Key pivots and what triggered them
- Connections that changed their trajectory
- Setbacks and how they handled them
- Reinvention moments

**Slide 8: Lessons**
"What I've learned from [Name]" with 2-3 numbered takeaways. Each lesson must be:
- Distinct from the others (not variations of the same point)
- Tied to a specific example from their story
- Tactical, not abstract

### Writing Principles

**Show, don't tell.** Let the facts speak for themselves. Don't add commentary explaining why something is impressive or what it means.

Bad: "She had to fight town regulations, persuade health departments, and deal with difficult neighbors. It was over a year of slow, difficult work before anything picked up. Most people would have given up. Martha kept going."

Good: "She had to fight town regulations, persuade health departments to let her cook from home, and deal with neighbors who didn't want a business operating on their street."

**Weave, don't list.** Connect events into a narrative flow. Avoid turning the carousel into a chronological list of disconnected facts.

Bad: "In 1987, Kmart signed her. In 1990, Time launched the magazine. In 1993, she did a TV show. In 1995, she had four million books."

Good: "Kmart signed her to a $5 million consulting contract. Time launched Martha Stewart Living magazine. In 1993, she debuted a syndicated TV show. By 1995, she had four million books in print and the magazine was selling over a million copies per issue."

**Dense slides.** Each slide should have real substance—multiple sentences with specific details. Not sparse fragments.

### AI Patterns to Avoid in Biographical Content

- "Not because X, but because Y" constructions
- "On paper... In Reality..." contrasts
- "This wasn't some X" phrases
- Em dashes for dramatic effect
- Choppy sentence fragments
- Rhetorical statements ("This is a pattern you'll see...")
- Commentary like "Most people would have given up"
- Editorializing about what facts "mean"

### Lessons Format

Three distinct, tactical takeaways. Each one should:
- Start with a bolded principle
- Follow with a specific example from the story
- Feel like something Alex could actually apply

**Good lessons:**
```
1. Taking a step back isn't always a step back. Leaving Wall Street for a basement catering business looked like career death but it was actually the foundation for a billion-dollar company.

2. Make connections and stay open to where they lead. A friend's friend helped her break into Wall Street. A conversation at a catering gig became a book deal. A cooking segment with Snoop Dogg became one of her most defining partnerships.

3. Keep reinventing yourself. Martha went from model to stockbroker to caterer to cookbook author to media CEO to federal inmate to comeback queen. She never treated any version of herself as the final one.
```

**Bad lessons:**
- Two lessons that are variations of "setbacks can be good"
- Abstract principles without specific examples
- Generic advice like "believe in yourself"

### Example: Martha Stewart Carousel

**Slide 1: Hook**
```
NONLINEAR career paths

wall street → catering → media business →
America's first self-made female billionaire

MARTHA STEWART
```

**Slide 2: modeling → wall street**
```
Martha started modeling in high school to pay for college and support her family.

After college, she went to Wall Street as a stockbroker after a mutual friend introduced her to her future boss.

She left when a recession hit and moved with her husband to Westport, Connecticut, where they bought and renovated an old farmhouse.
```

**Slide 3: catering era**
```
She started a catering company called Uncatered Affair out of her basement with Norma Collier, a friend from her modeling days. The partnership fell apart after conflicts between them, and Martha continued on her own.

She had to fight town regulations, persuade health departments to let her cook from home, and deal with neighbors who didn't want a business operating on their street.
```

**Slide 4: her first book**
```
While catering a book release party, she met Alan Mirken, head of Crown Publishing. He asked if she'd ever thought about writing a cookbook.

Two years later, she got a $25,000 advance from Crown. The result was Entertaining, published in 1982 with 300 recipes. It became a bestseller.
```

**Slide 5: empire-building**
```
Kmart signed her to a $5 million consulting contract. Time launched Martha Stewart Living magazine. In 1993, she debuted a syndicated TV show. By 1995, she had four million books in print and the magazine was selling over a million copies per issue.

She founded Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia and when the company went public, she became America's first self-made female billionaire.
```

**Slide 6: a big setback**
```
In 2004, Martha was convicted of lying to federal investigators about a stock sale and sentenced to five months in prison. She requested to start her sentence immediately and entered a minimum-security facility.

Her prison nickname was M. Diddy, and she spent her time teaching other female inmates how to start businesses.

While she was inside, her company's stock price quadrupled.
```

**Slide 7: reinvention**
```
After her release, Martha immediately launched two new TV shows. Snoop Dogg appeared on The Martha Stewart Show to learn how to make mashed potatoes. Martha said she liked his giggle and his "quest for learning"- it was the start of an iconic friendship.

In 2023, Martha became the oldest Sports Illustrated Swimsuit cover model at 81.
```

**Slide 8: what i've learned from MARTHA**
```
1. Taking a step back isn't always a step back. Leaving Wall Street for a basement catering business looked like career death but it was actually the foundation for a billion-dollar company.

2. Make connections and stay open to where they lead. A friend's friend helped her break into Wall Street. A conversation at a catering gig became a book deal. A cooking segment with Snoop Dogg became one of her most defining partnerships.

3. Keep reinventing yourself. Martha went from model to stockbroker to caterer to cookbook author to media CEO to federal inmate to comeback queen. She never treated any version of herself as the final one.
```
