# AI Writing Patterns to Avoid

This is a comprehensive list of writing patterns that immediately signal "AI wrote this." Never use these in Hannah's newsletter writing.

## 1. Contrast Framing Overload

**Pattern**: "It's not X, it's Y" structure used constantly

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "Marketing isn't about selling, it's about connecting."
- "Success isn't luck, it's preparation."
- "It's not about the destination, it's about the journey"

**Why it's bad**: Humans don't speak in constant philosophical opposites. It sounds preachy and reductive.

**What to do instead**: State things directly. If you need contrast, use it once maximum per piece, and make it specific to the situation.

## 2. Rule of Three Everywhere

**Pattern**: Grouping everything in threes because it's "memorable"

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "Efficient, effective, and reliable"
- "Save time, reduce costs, increase ROI"
- "Simple, powerful, transformative"
- Lists of exactly three items when 2 or 4 would be more natural

**Why it's bad**: It turns writing into a jingle. Feels manufactured.

**What to do instead**: Use the Rule of Three extremely sparingly. Let the natural number of points dictate the structure.

## 3. Cringe Transition Questions

**Pattern**: Faux-engagement questions that sound like infomercials

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "The catch?"
- "The kicker?"
- "The brutal truth?"
- "Want to know the secret?"
- "But wait, there's more!"
- "Sound familiar?"

**Why it's bad**: Reeks of bad marketing copy. Condescending to readers.

**What to do instead**: If you wouldn't ask it out loud in conversation, don't write it. Use natural transitions or just move to the next point.

## 4. Present-ing Verbs (Corporate Speak)

**Pattern**: Overuse of -ing verbs that sound like business manuals

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "...highlighting key benefits..."
- "...emphasizing the importance..."
- "...facilitating enhanced collaboration..."
- "...driving meaningful impact..."
- "...leveraging existing resources..."

**Why it's bad**: Sounds like it was written by a committee. Removes all personality.

**What to do instead**: Use simple, active verbs. "Shows" not "highlighting." "Helps" not "facilitating."

## 5. Vague Glazing Opinions

**Pattern**: Throat-clearing before making a point

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "It's worth considering..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- "One might argue that..."
- "It could be said that..."
- "You're really getting at something special here..."

**Why it's bad**: Hedges everything. Shows no conviction. Wastes words.

**What to do instead**: Just state your opinion directly. Have a point of view.

## 6. Formality First Language

**Pattern**: Using $4 words when 50-cent words work better

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "Utilize" → use "use"
- "Execute" → use "do"
- "Facilitate" → use "help"
- "Expedite" → use "speed up"
- "Address" → use "fix" or "talk about"
- "Synergy" → use "working together"
- "Implement" → use "start" or "put in place"
- "Optimize" → use "improve" or "make better"
- "Commence" → use "start"
- "Ascertain" → use "find out"

**Why it's bad**: Creates distance. Sounds corporate and stiff.

**What to do instead**: Write like you talk. Use simple, direct language.

## 7. Emoji Explosion

**Pattern**: Emojis scattered throughout like confetti

**Examples to AVOID**:
- 🚀 Boost your productivity!
- ✨ Transform your business today!
- 💡 Here's a brilliant idea!
- Using 3+ emojis in a single paragraph

**Why it's bad**: Looks like spam. Cheapens the message.

**What to do instead**: Use emojis very sparingly. Hannah occasionally uses them but not as decoration.

## 8. Extra Em Dashes

**Pattern**: Em dash (—) overuse in every other sentence

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "The solution was simple—yet powerful—and exactly what we needed."
- Using em dashes in 3+ consecutive sentences
- Em dashes where commas or periods would work fine

**Why it's bad**: AI overuses them. Creates a predictable rhythm.

**What to do instead**: Mix up punctuation. Use commas, periods, parentheses. Em dashes are fine occasionally but not as a default.

## 9. Everything Is "Symbolic"

**Pattern**: Telling readers what things "represent" instead of what happened

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "This represents a shift in..."
- "This symbolizes the importance of..."
- "This reflects a deeper truth about..."
- "This indicates that..."
- "This emphasizes the need for..."

**Why it's bad**: Over-interpretation. Preachy. Tells instead of shows.

**What to do instead**: State what happened or what you learned. Let readers draw their own conclusions.

## 10. Made-Up People (The "Sarah Chen" Problem)

**Pattern**: Creating fictional examples with generic multicultural names when no real data exists

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "Sarah Chen, a product manager at a Series B startup, struggled with..."
- "Meet Alex Rodriguez, who tripled his income by..."
- Any detailed fictional case study presented as if it's real

**Why it's bad**: Feels fake. Readers know you're making it up.

**What to do instead**: Use real examples from your own experience, acknowledge when something is hypothetical, or use simple "imagine if" framing.

## 11. Choppy Dramatic Fragments

**Pattern**: Breaking a single thought into multiple short sentences for theatrical effect

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "Not for tech Twitter. For his parents."
- "Not in ten years. In one to five."
- "No edits." as its own beat after a longer sentence

**Why it's bad**: It's a writing trick that AI overuses. It creates false drama and a staccato rhythm that doesn't match how Hannah actually writes. She uses short sentences sometimes, but not as a constant rhetorical device to create punchy beats.

**What to do instead**: Connect the ideas naturally with commas or dashes. "Shumer wrote this for his parents, not for tech Twitter" reads like a person talking. "Shumer wrote this for his parents. Not for tech Twitter." reads like a copywriter.

## 11b. "Not X, but Y" as a Standalone Construction

**Pattern**: Using "Not because X, but because Y" or "Not by choice, but because..." as a dramatic standalone sentence or fragment

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "Not because they chose it, but because the linear path stopped existing."
- "Not by choice. Because the linear path is going away."
- "Not in ten years. In one to five."

**Why it's bad**: This is a dead AI tell. It sounds like a TED talk punchline. When used as a standalone beat or sentence-ender, it creates artificial drama. Hannah states things directly.

**What to do instead**: Fold the idea into a natural sentence. "Everyone will have a nonlinear career, even if they didn't choose one, because the linear path stops existing" reads like a person with a point of view. The "not X but Y" construction as a standalone beat reads like a copywriter.

## 12. Slang and Phrases Hannah Doesn't Use

**Pattern**: AI-generated casual language that sounds forced

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "hit different"
- "the real punchline" — too performative, just make the point
- "And honestly?" as a rhetorical beat
- "sing-songy" emotional beats like "I know that's a lot. But honestly? For a lot of you..."
- Any rhetorical questions used as transitions or emotional beats

**Why it's bad**: These are AI's attempt at sounding casual. They come across as performative rather than natural. Hannah's casual voice uses "kind of," "like," parenthetical asides, and direct statements. She doesn't do theatrical pauses or emotional hand-holding.

**What to do instead**: Just say the thing directly. If it's a big statement, let the content carry the weight. Don't add a beat before it to tell the reader how to feel.

## 12. Adverb Overload

**Pattern**: Leaning on adverbs instead of letting the point speak for itself

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "quietly shifting"
- "genuinely cannot"
- "deeply technical"
- "fundamentally changed"
- "quickly realized"

**Why it's bad**: Adverbs pad sentences without adding meaning. Hannah prefers fewer, more precise words. Most adverbs can be cut and the sentence gets better, not worse.

**What to do instead**: Cut the adverb and see if the sentence still works. It usually does. If the verb isn't strong enough on its own, pick a better verb.

## 13. Speaking About Audience in Third Person

**Pattern**: Referring to the reader as "those people" or "people who" instead of "you"

**Examples to AVOID**:
- "people who are stuck in traditional industries"
- "ambitious people in finance and consulting"
- "the people I talk to every day"
- "for those people, I think this is good news"

**Why it's bad**: Creates distance. Hannah writes TO her audience, not ABOUT them. Her readers are "you," not "them."

**What to do instead**: Address the reader directly. "A lot of you are in finance, consulting..." not "A lot of people I talk to are in finance, consulting..." When referencing outside content about her audience, translate it into second person.

## 14. Paraphrasing Without Quoting

**Pattern**: Rewording someone's ideas closely enough that it's basically copying, but without quotation marks

**Examples to AVOID**:
- Restating an article's points in slightly different words and presenting them as your own summary
- Taking someone's argument and just lightly rearranging the sentences

**Why it's bad**: It's lazy, it's potentially plagiarism, and it removes Hannah's own perspective from the writing. If Hannah is referencing someone else's work, the value she adds is her opinion and how it connects to her audience, not a reworded version of what they said.

**What to do instead**: Use actual quotation marks when pulling someone's words. Link to the source. Then add your own take. The ratio should be: short quote + your reaction/opinion, not: long paraphrase of their whole argument.

## 15. Redundant Framing

**Pattern**: Saying what you're about to say before saying it, or restating your thesis in slightly different words multiple times

**Examples to AVOID**:
- A TLDR that basically repeats the first few paragraphs
- "The real punchline" section that restates the conclusion you already made
- "Here's what I'd actually do" when you've already been saying what to do
- Section headers that announce what the reader is about to read instead of adding meaning

**Why it's bad**: It pads word count and treats the reader like they weren't paying attention. Hannah's readers are smart. They got it the first time.

**What to do instead**: Say it once, say it well. If a section header or TLDR is just repeating what follows, cut it. If the conclusion restates the intro, make the conclusion add something new or make it very concise.

## Quick Detection Test

Read the draft aloud. If it sounds like:
- A LinkedIn thought leader
- A corporate training manual
- An infomercial
- Someone trying too hard to sound smart
- Someone who learned English from business textbooks

...then it's AI slop. Rewrite it.

## The Golden Rule

**If you wouldn't say it in a conversation with a smart friend over coffee, don't write it.**
