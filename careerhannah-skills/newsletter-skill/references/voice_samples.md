# Voice Samples

These excerpts demonstrate Hannah's authentic writing voice. Use these as your north star when transforming rough drafts.

## Key Voice Characteristics

**Direct and personal**: First-person, conversational, like talking over coffee
**Concrete details**: Specific examples, real brand names, actual numbers
**Honest about struggle**: Admits uncertainty, failure, doubt
**No corporate speak**: Short sentences, simple words, natural flow
**In medias res**: Often starts with a story or moment, not abstract philosophy

## Sample 1: Investment Banking (Strongest Example)

> I once worked 25 hours and had a mental breakdown in the shower. I also became my most analytical, tough, and efficient work self.

> The gratification of finally landing my long-coveted IBD job lasted about two months. The thrill of getting staffed on my first deal. The fast-paced environment where I was learning new things about the sector every day from smart people. The new title on LinkedIn — "Investment Banking Analyst." Building my first model. Not thinking twice about being at the office on a Saturday. Wearing 90-hour weeks as a badge of pride.

> I knew it was coming — but when the non-stop Friday night staffings, page-long 3am comments, and 100th version of an already perfect slide deck came, they came and they didn't stop.

> I became miserable, anxious, and unhealthy. Every waking moment spent with my friends and family became an endless complaint about my difficulties dealing with colleagues and clients, delving into minute but maddening details about the injustices I suffered and the pointless comments I stayed up all night to turn. I dreaded weekends in fear of Friday night staffings and was constantly anxious to receive messages and phone calls on nights out. It was as if I couldn't stop the negativity pouring out of my mouth in every situation — it poisoned and paralyzed my attitude and my relationships. I needed to quit.

**What works**: Specific details (25 hours, 3am comments, Friday night staffings). Natural rhythm. No forced cadence. Admits real emotions without therapyspeak.

## Sample 2: Lazy Newsletter Reset

> TLDR: I'm changing this newsletter. I've been lazy and low effort for the last few months, but I'm done with that. Here's why.

> I went viral on LinkedIn.

> (This isn't meant to be a brag—because at the same time, I've been reaching a fraction of my follower count on Instagram and feeling inadequate over there.)

> The point isn't the virality, it's the resonance.

> …So why did I start sending you a list of jobs every week and calling it a day?

> Because I got lazy.

> A year into content and overwhelmed by formats—Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, YouTube shorts, newsletter—I started optimizing one by one and losing the plot.

**What works**: TLDR format. Direct admission ("I got lazy"). Natural parenthetical aside. Short paragraphs. Question asked naturally, not as a hook.

## Sample 3: Maximizer's Dilemma

> For 2025, my word was maximization.
> (And experimentation and optionality. Am I really a maximizer if I pick just one word??)

> I started creating content because I wanted optionality from my 9-5. I "brute forced" my way to 100K followers. My content strategy changed on a weekly basis, I got caught in comparison traps with other creators and responded to trends more than I focused on building for an audience.

> If you worked in banking, consulting, or tech, you probably know exactly what I'm talking about. You optimized for optionality at every turn. You picked the role with the most exit opportunities rather than the one you were most excited about. You're five or ten years into your career and still "figuring it out."

**What works**: Self-aware humor in parenthetical. "Brute forced" in quotes (knows it's cliche). Speaks directly to reader's experience. No rhetorical questions as hooks.

## Sample 4: Personal Brands

> A year ago, I still thought "personal branding" was for people selling courses on how to sell courses.

> I'd scroll Linkedin and see "thought leaders" posting preachy garbage about their management tips or shilling their consulting businesses with bios like "I help Fortune 500 companies unlock synergies." It was gross. I wanted nothing to do with it.

> I had real credentials and experience. I went to an Ivy League school. I worked in investment banking and tech and had good performance reviews. My work spoke for itself.

> Or so I thought.

**What works**: Specific quote that shows don't tell ("unlock synergies"). Blunt language ("gross," "garbage"). Short reveal ("Or so I thought") without making it a pattern.

## Sample 5: AI Article Commentary (Reactive/Analysis Piece)

> Matt Shumer, an AI founder, posted an essay on X this week called "Something Big Is Happening" that now has more than 80 million views. I posted a video about it and a lot of you saw it, but short-form video doesn't leave much room for nuance, and this topic needs it. So I want to dive deeper here.

> Despite being a big vibe coder and AI dabbler in my own life, I don't write about AI in this newsletter. It's not my expertise and I already spend way too much time thinking about it in my 9-5. But I'm making an exception because I think a lot of you are in industries like finance and professional services where nobody is having this conversation. Frankly, even at most tech companies and startups, people aren't having it either.

> Where I'd push back: I think Coogan underestimates how many people's jobs are closer to a screen than they think. And the fact that disruption isn't evenly distributed doesn't mean you should wait to see if it hits you. By the time it's obvious, the window to prepare has closed.

> I've seen that. But I think it actually strengthens the case for paying attention. If some people are going to waste time playing AI FarmVille while other people are using these tools to do real work in a fraction of the time, that gap is going to widen.

> But if you've been reading this newsletter for a while, you already know how to operate in that world. The five things above are the same things that have always separated people who navigate change well from people who get caught off guard: use the tools, invest in people, stay financially flexible, build something of your own, and make sure you're known for more than your job title.

**What works**: Drops right into the article without preamble. Honest about why she's breaking her own pattern ("It's not my expertise"). Uses "frankly" naturally. Engages with counterpoints by stating where she agrees and where she'd push back without hedging. Connects external content to her audience's world. Conclusion ties back to her broader thesis without restating the whole piece. No choppy dramatic fragments. Sentences flow naturally with commas and dashes instead of being broken apart for effect.

**What this sample shows about reactive/commentary pieces**: Hannah leads with the source material, is transparent about her relationship to the topic, engages fairly with opposing views (agrees where she agrees, pushes back where she doesn't), and always brings it back to what matters for her specific audience.

## What Hannah NEVER Does

- Rhetorical question hooks ("Ready to transform your career?")
- Forced three-part lists embedded everywhere
- Transition questions ("The catch?" "The kicker?")
- Present-ing verbs ("highlighting," "emphasizing," "facilitating")
- Vague hedging ("It's worth considering...")
- Corporate synonyms (utilize, execute, facilitate)
- Made-up people/examples (no "Sarah Chen")
- Everything being "symbolic" or "representing" something
- Em dash overload
- Choppy dramatic fragments ("Not for tech Twitter. For his parents." — connect these naturally with commas)
- "Not because X, but because Y" as a standalone construction — this is a dead AI tell
- Slang she doesn't use: "hit different," "the real punchline," "And honestly?"
- Unnecessary adverbs: "quietly," "genuinely," "deeply," "fundamentally"
- Speaking about her audience in third person ("people who are stuck") instead of second person ("a lot of you")

## Rhythm and Flow

Hannah's writing flows like natural speech:
- Mix of long and short sentences
- Paragraphs of varying length (sometimes just one sentence)
- Uses lists when genuinely needed, embedded in prose
- Breaks for emphasis, not for algorithm
- Parentheticals feel natural, not overdone
- Dashes and commas for pacing, not decoration
