# Nonlinear Career Paths Skill

Take a person's name, research their full career story, and produce a finished carousel in the Nonlinear Career Paths format.

This is a research-first skill. Do not draft slides until you have real facts, real names, real numbers, and a clear narrative arc. Generic storytelling is worse than no storytelling.

---

## Step 0: Who Qualifies

Good subjects have at least one of these:

- Left something prestigious (finance, consulting, law, medicine, top corp) to do something unexpected
- Built something real alongside a serious career — ran both lanes simultaneously before choosing
- Had a visible failure or setback that most people would have quit over
- Reinvented themselves more than once across completely different fields
- Broke into something that looked impossible from where they started
- Their path looks like a mess from the outside but makes total sense in hindsight
- Shows that nonlinear is strategic — they didn't abandon one lane, they compounded across multiple

**The test:** Would Alex (28-32, elite credentials, has a 9-5 AND building something on the side, wants the day job and the brand and the income streams to compound) look at this person and feel something? Either "that could be me" or "I never would have had the guts." If the answer is yes to either, the person qualifies.

The person does not have to come from banking or consulting. Former athletes, musicians, chefs, artists, scientists, founders, academics — anyone whose career defies a straight line works.

---

## Step 1: Research

Use web search to build a fact base before writing a single word. You are looking for:

**Career facts:**
- Every job, role, or industry they worked in (with approximate dates)
- What they left to pursue the next thing — and why
- Specific dollar amounts (salaries, deals, advances, valuations)
- Company names, school names, publication names
- Names of key people who influenced or opened doors for them
- The pivotal moment or decision that changed everything

**Story texture:**
- A detail that most people don't know
- A failure or rejection that happened before the breakthrough
- Something they said publicly about why they made a switch
- A moment of doubt, hesitation, or almost giving up
- What they were doing just before their big break

**Outcome facts:**
- What they built, sold, or became known for
- Numbers that prove scale (revenue, users, copies sold, awards)
- Where they are now

**Verification:**
Fact-check every claim you intend to use. If you can't verify a specific number or name, cut it — don't approximate or assume.

---

## Step 2: Find the Arc

Before writing slides, map the career story as a sequence of eras. Each era should:
- Cover a distinct phase (not just a year or a job title)
- Have a clear "why it matters to the story" reason for being there
- Connect to the next era with a cause or consequence

Most stories have 4-6 eras. Don't force more. If two phases blur together, combine them.

**Good era names** are lowercase, evocative, and feel like a chapter title:
- "the accounting years"
- "catering era"
- "the in-between"
- "her first book"
- "a big setback"
- "the pivot"

**Bad era names:**
- "early career" (too generic)
- "success" (too vague)
- "2014-2018" (dates only, no story signal)
- "The Rise of [Name]" (too dramatic/AI-ish)

---

## Step 3: Cover Slide

The cover is the most important slide. It determines whether anyone swipes.

There are two cover formats. **Use Format B (narrative headline) by default** — it consistently outperforms Format A because it sells the story, not just the sequence.

---

### Format A: Trajectory Cover
Use when the career arc itself is so wild that the simple sequence IS the hook (John Summit, Martha Stewart style).

```
NONLINEAR career paths   ← large, bold, prominent

[role] → [role] → [role] → [outcome]   ← lowercase trajectory line

[PERSON NAME]   ← name pill/badge
```

**Example (John Summit):**
```
NONLINEAR career paths

accountant → fired → headlining DJ selling out Madison Square Garden

JOHN SUMMIT
```

---

### Format B: Narrative Headline Cover (DEFAULT)
Use this as the default. Works for anyone. Tells the story in one sentence so the reader knows immediately what they're getting. (Payal Kadakia style — highest-performing cover format.)

```
NONLINEAR CAREER PATHS   ← smaller subtitle text

[One sentence that describes what makes this person's story interesting]   ← large, prominent headline

[company/brand logos if relevant]   ← optional, adds credibility

[PERSON NAME]   ← name pill/badge
```

**Example (Payal Kadakia):**
```
NONLINEAR CAREER PATHS

How a Bain consultant quit to build ClassPass into a billion-dollar company

[Bain logo] [ClassPass logo]

PAYAL KADAKIA
```

**How to write the narrative headline:**
- Lead with who they were (the prestigious/recognizable starting point)
- Include the action they took (quit, left, walked away from)
- End with what they built or became (the outcome that proves it worked)
- Keep it to one sentence, under 20 words
- Use specific nouns — company names, dollar amounts, outcomes — not adjectives

**Good:** "How a Harvard-trained surgeon left medicine to build a billion-dollar health tech company"
**Bad:** "How one woman's unconventional path led to incredible success"

---

## Step 4: Middle Slides (Era Slides)

Each middle slide follows this structure:

```
[era name]   ← lowercase, bold, in the style color (era title)

[Narrative text: 2-4 sentences, dense with specifics]

[Narrative text: 1-3 more sentences continuing the story]
```

**Visual format note:** Text appears in a rounded white text box overlaid on a photo of the person from that era or relevant to that moment. This is the consistent visual identity of the series.

**Target: 6 era slides for most stories. Minimum 4, maximum 7.**

---

### Writing the Slide Text

**Show, don't tell.** Let the facts speak. Do not explain why something is impressive or add commentary about what it means.

Bad:
> "She had to fight town regulations, persuade health departments, and deal with difficult neighbors. It was over a year of slow, difficult work before anything picked up. Most people would have given up. Martha kept going."

Good:
> "She had to fight town regulations, persuade health departments to let her cook from home, and deal with neighbors who didn't want a business operating on their street."

The second version is shorter and more powerful because it trusts the reader to feel the difficulty.

---

**Weave, don't list.** Connect events into narrative flow. Do not write a chronological list of disconnected facts.

Bad:
> "In 1987, Kmart signed her. In 1990, Time launched the magazine. In 1993, she did a TV show."

Good:
> "Kmart signed her to a $5 million consulting contract. Time launched Martha Stewart Living magazine. In 1993, she debuted a syndicated TV show. By 1995, she had four million books in print and the magazine was selling over a million copies per issue."

---

**Dense slides.** Each slide should have 3-5 sentences with real substance. Not fragments, not bullets, not thin observations.

Bad:
> "He kept playing. He kept getting better. People started to notice."

Good:
> "He started playing small clubs in Chicago while still working as an accountant. His first few years of bookings came entirely through connections he made by showing up — no manager, no label, just gigs. The first time he played to a crowd over 500 people, he called in sick to work the next day."

---

**One story per slide.** Each slide covers one era. Don't jump ahead.

---

## Step 5: Final Slide

```
what i've learned from [FIRST NAME or SHORT NAME]   ← bold, contrasting background (blue)

1. [Bold principle.] [Specific example from their story that proves it.]

2. [Bold principle.] [Specific example from their story that proves it.]

3. [Bold principle.] [Specific example from their story that proves it.]
```

**Rules for the three lessons:**

- Each lesson must be **distinct** — not three variations of "setbacks can be good"
- Each lesson must connect to a **specific moment** in the story you told — not generic wisdom
- Each lesson should feel **applicable to Alex** — the ambitious 28-32 year old building something alongside their 9-5, who wants multiple lanes to compound and needs proof that nonlinear is strategic, not reckless
- Format: **Bold principle.** Followed by the story evidence in the same sentence or the next
- No generic advice ("believe in yourself," "stay the course," "embrace uncertainty")

**Good lessons (Martha Stewart):**
```
1. Taking a step back isn't always a step back. Leaving Wall Street for a basement catering business looked like career death but it was actually the foundation for a billion-dollar company.

2. Make connections and stay open to where they lead. A friend's friend helped her break into Wall Street. A conversation at a catering gig became a book deal. A cooking segment with Snoop Dogg became one of her most defining partnerships.

3. Keep reinventing yourself. Martha went from model to stockbroker to caterer to cookbook author to media CEO to federal inmate to comeback queen. She never treated any version of herself as the final one.
```

**Bad lessons:**
- "Follow your passion even when it's hard" (generic, not tied to their story)
- Two lessons that both say "failure leads to growth" (not distinct)
- A lesson that contradicts the actual story

## AI Patterns to Avoid in Biographical Content

The following patterns make biographical carousels sound AI-generated. Cut them on sight:

- "Not because X, but because Y" constructions
- "On paper... In Reality..." contrasts
- "This wasn't some X" phrases
- Em dashes for dramatic effect
- Choppy sentence fragments used as "punchy" writing
- Rhetorical statements ("This is a pattern you'll see...")
- Commentary: "Most people would have given up," "Few people would have had the courage"
- Editorializing: "This was a pivotal moment," "Everything changed after this"
- Vague superlatives: "incredibly," "remarkable," "extraordinary"

The story should be remarkable. You should not say that it is.

## Self-Critique Checklist

Before delivering:

1. **Is every fact verifiable?** No approximations, no assumptions. If in doubt, cut it.
2. **Is the cover doing its job?** Would someone stop scrolling because of slide 1 alone?
3. **Does each era slide have real substance?** At least 3 sentences, at least 2 specific details (name, number, place, quote).
4. **Is the writing narrative, not list-like?** Read each slide out loud. Does it flow?
5. **Are the three lessons distinct, story-specific, and applicable to Alex?**
6. **Zero AI tells?** No em dashes, no "not X but Y," no commentary about what facts mean.
7. **Would someone who knows this person's story recognize it?** That's the bar for accuracy.
