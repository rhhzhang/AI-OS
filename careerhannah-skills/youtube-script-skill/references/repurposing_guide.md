# Repurposing Guide: Source Material to YouTube Script

This file covers how to transform different types of source material into YouTube scripts for Hannah. Each source type has different strengths and requires different adaptation.

## Table of contents

1. Newsletter to YouTube
2. Raw idea or braindump to YouTube
3. Reel script to YouTube (long-form expansion)
4. External article or trend to YouTube (reactive/commentary)
5. Interview or conversation to YouTube

---

## 1. Newsletter to YouTube

Newsletters are Hannah's richest source material. They're already in her voice, have real arguments, personal stories, data, and external references. But they need heavy restructuring for video.

### What to keep from the newsletter

- Hannah's original language and phrasing (don't rewrite her voice)
- Specific data points, examples, and references (NY Mag salaries, Naval quotes, etc.)
- Personal stories and anecdotes (these become mid-video proof, not hooks)
- The core thesis and argument structure
- Her opinions and where she pushes back on conventional thinking

### What to cut or restructure

- The newsletter opening. It's written for subscribers who already know Hannah. YouTube needs a stranger-friendly hook about the VIEWER's problem.
- Multiple threads. A 1500-word newsletter often has 3+ distinct arguments. Pick ONE throughline for one YouTube video. Save the rest for other videos.
- Extended quotes. In the newsletter, Hannah can quote Naval at length or cite David Brooks with a link. On video, this becomes brief paraphrase in her own words. One sentence, attribute, move on.
- Listicle sections. "5 principles for X" at the end of a newsletter becomes its own separate video, or gets cut entirely. One video, one thesis.
- Links and footnotes. Can't click on video. Translate into "I'll link this in the description" or just paraphrase.
- Job listings and footer content. Obviously doesn't transfer.

### The adaptation process

Step 1: Identify the single strongest throughline. Read the newsletter and ask: if someone watched a 10-minute video about this, what's the ONE thing they should walk away with? That's the throughline. Everything else is either supporting material or a separate video.

Step 2: Find the hook in the viewer's problem. The newsletter usually starts with Hannah's personal experience. For YouTube, flip it: what's the viewer's version of this problem? "The highest achieving people I know are stuck in careers that look impressive but aren't making them wealthy" is the viewer's version of the wedding story.

Step 3: Map the 5-section structure. Hook (viewer's problem) > Setup (introduce the framework) > Body (build the argument with 3-4 payoffs) > "For You" pivot (connect to the viewer's life) > Reframe (land one sharp idea, not a summary).

Step 4: Place personal stories as mid-video proof. The wedding story moves to minute 6:45 where it works as evidence that the thesis is real. Personal stories earn more trust mid-video when the viewer already understands the stakes.

Step 5: Plant open loops at section transitions. Every section should resolve one question while opening the next. Don't let the viewer feel like they've gotten the full picture until the end.

### Typical yield

One 1500-word newsletter usually produces 1-2 YouTube videos (one from the main thesis, potentially one from a tactical section like "5 principles for X"). Occasionally a rich newsletter produces 3, but don't force it.

## 2. Raw idea or braindump to YouTube

Hannah sometimes has a thesis or observation that hasn't been written up yet. These need more development than newsletter repurposing.

### Questions to ask before writing

- What's the personal experience or observation behind this idea?
- Who specifically is this for? Someone still in corporate, someone who just left, someone building?
- Is there a framework, matrix, or mental model that can be introduced?
- What's the contrarian angle? Hannah's best content challenges a common assumption.
- Is there specific data, a recent article, or a trend that anchors this?
- What's the ONE thing the viewer should walk away thinking or doing differently?

If any of these are thin, ask Hannah before writing. Don't fill gaps with generic advice or made-up examples.

### Development process

Step 1: Artist pass. Get all of Hannah's raw thinking down. Bullet points, half-formed thoughts, connections she's making. Don't structure yet.

Step 2: Architect pass. Find the throughline. Find the hook (viewer's problem). Map the 5-section structure. Identify where data or examples are needed.

Step 3: Writer pass. Draft the full script. Connect sections with natural transitions. Make sure something is progressing, regressing, or changing in every section.

Step 4: Wizard pass. Check retention. Where will people drop off? Add open loops. Make sure the hook earns the setup, the setup earns the body, and so on.

## 3. Reel script to YouTube (long-form expansion)

Reels are compressed. A YouTube video is the expanded version.

### What changes

- The reel hook often works as a YouTube hook (check if it's specific enough for a stranger)
- Expand on points that were compressed for short-form (add the "why," the data, the personal story)
- Add context that visuals provided in the reel (describe what the viewer would have seen)
- Add the sections that short-form can't accommodate: historical context, multiple examples, the "for you" pivot
- The punchline or CTA from the reel often becomes the reframe at the end of the YouTube video

### What stays the same

- The core thesis
- Hannah's framing and language
- The audience pain point the reel targeted

## 4. External article or trend to YouTube (reactive/commentary)

Hannah's newsletter on the Matt Shumer AI post is the model for this format. It's also how she'd react to NY Mag salaries, a viral LinkedIn post, or a major industry shift.

### Structure for reactive videos

Hook: State the thing that happened and why it matters to the viewer. Not "Matt Shumer posted an essay" but "An AI founder just said most white-collar jobs will be automated in 5 years. Here's what he gets right and what he gets wrong."

Context: Brief summary of the source material. Quote the key claim directly (with attribution). Don't paraphrase the whole thing.

Hannah's take: Where she agrees and where she pushes back. This is the meat. She needs to add perspective, not just summarize. Connect the source material to her audience's world.

Implications for the viewer: What does this mean for someone in finance, consulting, tech? What should they do about it?

Reframe: Land on one sharp observation that ties the source material to Hannah's broader thesis (nonlinear careers, personal brands, the status-wealth gap, etc.).

### The critical thing

Hannah must add her OWN perspective. A reactive video that just summarizes someone else's argument is worthless. The value is Hannah's take, Hannah's pushback, and how she connects it to her audience.

## 5. Interview or conversation to YouTube

If Hannah does YouTube interviews or podcast-style conversations, the approach is different.

### Before the interview

Use the interview-writing-skill to create the brief. The brief should flow like a conversation, not an interrogation. Questions should lead the guest to insights naturally.

### After the interview

The raw conversation needs heavy editing for YouTube. Find the 3-4 best moments and build the video around those. The hook should be the single most surprising or compelling thing the guest said.

Structure: Hook (best moment from interview) > Brief intro of who this person is and why they're interesting > The conversation, edited for pacing > Hannah's takeaway or reaction at the end.

---

## General principles for all source types

1. One video, one throughline. Don't try to cover everything.
2. Hook is about the viewer's problem, not about Hannah.
3. Personal stories go in the middle as proof, not at the top as intros.
4. Every section earns the next section. Open loops at transitions.
5. Write it to be spoken, not read. If a sentence sounds stiff out loud, rewrite it.
6. Keep Hannah's original language. Don't "improve" her phrasing.
7. Cut anything a stranger wouldn't care about (subscriber-only context, inside jokes, links).
8. Run the humanizer on every finished script. Read every line and ask "would she say this?"
