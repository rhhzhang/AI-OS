# Example: Hannah's MBA Video (Best Performing)

This is Hannah's best-performing YouTube video. Study this as the gold standard for how her YouTube scripts work. The script below is a mix of fully written sections and bullet-point sections that Hannah riffs on during filming. Both formats are valid in Hannah's scripts.

## Metadata

Title: Watch this before you get an MBA (I graduated from Wharton 1 year ago)
Runtime: 17:29
Format: Talking head, longer format, high detail

## Thumbnail concept

Hannah in a white t-shirt, chin resting on hand (thinking pose), looking directly at camera. Bold text: "MBA: WORTH IT?" in large white block letters. Three school crests (Harvard, Stanford, Wharton) stacked on the right side. Campus building in the background. The thumbnail communicates: real person, big question, elite schools. It's clean, not cluttered, and the text is readable at small sizes.

## Why this video works

**The hook is contrarian and credentialed.** "Don't believe anyone on the internet who says 'an MBA is definitely worth it' or 'you should never get an MBA'" immediately disqualifies the two loudest camps and positions Hannah as the nuanced middle. She then establishes credibility (Wharton, last year) and sets the frame: this is honest, personal, and not trying to sell you anything.

**The structure is a framework, not a listicle.** She gives the viewer three questions to ask themselves, in order of importance. This is a decision-making tool, not a list of tips. The viewer walks away with something they can use.

**The personal story goes deep.** She doesn't just say "I went to Wharton." She talks about the breakup, the isolation in Latin America, the career confusion. She admits she applied because she thought it was a silver bullet. This level of honesty is what makes it work. No other creator in the MBA space is this raw.

**The "reasons NOT to" section is long, specific, and uncomfortable.** This is the retention engine. Each reason is something the viewer has probably thought about. The friends section is particularly strong because she goes against the MBA marketing ("I didn't feel like I had a single real friend" for months, "MBA has some of the worst people you will ever meet"). This is the kind of thing that makes someone screenshot and send to a friend.

**The answer at the end is "soft yes" with caveats.** She doesn't try to give a clean answer. The nuance IS the answer. This matches her brand: smart, honest, not trying to sell you a conclusion.

**The video is longer than the 8-12 minute target** (17:29). This works because the topic demands depth and the viewer is making a $200K decision. Not every video should be this long, but when the topic is high-stakes and personal, longer format is justified.

## Description

If you ask the internet, "Is an MBA worth it?" you'll typically get 1 of these answers: "It's a scam!" "It's the best decision I ever made!" But tbh these takes are missing a lot of context.

I graduated from Wharton last year, and this video is the honest breakdown I wish I had before dropping $250K and hitting pause on my career for two years.

I'm walking through:
- Why I actually went to business school
- The 3 questions I wish I asked myself before applying
- A long list of reasons NOT to go
- How it played out for me, and whether I think it was worth it

This isn't about convincing you one way or the other. It's about helping you think clearly before making a huge decision. Especially if you're feeling lost in your career, burnt out, or tempted to get an MBA just because everyone else is doing it.

Both approaches are valid. The fully written sections are moments where word choice and delivery matter (the hook, vulnerable admissions, sharp one-liners). The bullet-point sections are where Hannah's natural riffing adds energy and authenticity that a written script would flatten.

### HOOK

Don't believe anyone on the internet who says "an MBA is definitely worth it" or "you should never get an MBA it's a waste or money" because they're usually trying to sell you admissions consulting, or they wanted to get an MBA, didn't, and want to justify their own decision not to.

I graduated from Wharton last year and so all of this is speaking from my own experience and talking to friends both from business school and people who didn't go to business school.

Getting an MBA is not going to dramatically make your career or life better. But that doesn't mean you absolutely shouldn't get one or it isn't worth it. I know the internet doesn't love nuance but the real answer is - it depends on your personal situation and goals.

I believe there are 3 things you should ask yourself before you decide to go to business school and I'm going to go through how you can answer these 3 questions for yourself in this video.

### SETUP / ROADMAP

First I'm going to tell you a little bit about my story and why i decided to get an MBA, because everyone's situation is going to be different. I'm also going to talk about reasons NOT to get an MBA - this is a long list and you might feel attacked... caveats and disclaimers and exceptions to what I say, and how things played out for me and whether I think it was personally worth it for me. With also some stories about the reality of going to business school.

And just to be clear - I'm talking top 25 ranked MBA programs in the US. May apply to certain European MBAs as well, not to be elitist but just saying if you're going to get an expensive degree, in most cases it doesn't make sense for you to go unless it's top ranked.

### WHY I GOT AN MBA (personal story, fully written)

I'm gonna be really honest here - I started studying for the GMAT and applying to business school during a difficult period of my life. I had ended a long term relationship not that long ago, I was living abroad (I'm from the US but had moved to Latin America for a startup) and I felt really isolated. And I had just made a big career pivot from finance to tech. Basically I was all over the place and even though I had some good reasons to get an MBA which I'll get into... I applied to business school because I thought it was going to be a silver bullet to fix all of my problems. Like it costs $200k, how can it not give me more direction in my career, a bigger better network in the US, new friends... a higher salary??

[Then bullet-point riffing on specific reasons:]

- Wanted to go into product management bc my company at the time was very operational, didn't have a true product role... felt like product was more specialized, lot of hype around it. Big Tech name brand. Also kind of interested in VC, entrepreneurship
- Wanted a soft landing back in the US / build network. Got job offer but just felt like all my friends had scattered, didn't have a clear place I wanted to be, wanted opportunity to do internship, try out different cities
- Long term potential / insurance plan - different fields are becoming more competitive, having an MBA is kind of an insurance for the future, network to fall back on because it's another thing i can add to my resume
- Vibes. I felt a little lost, didn't know what I really wanted to do, felt lonely / isolated

### 3 QUESTIONS I WISH I ASKED MYSELF (framework section, bullet-point format for riffing)

**1. Do I need an MBA to change careers?**

- What role do you want next?
  - In my experience the careers you truly NEED business school to pivot to: investment banking, consulting
  - If you are coming from the military
- How does the MBA help you achieve that goal?
- If you don't get an MBA, what would you do to achieve that goal?
- Would an MBA significantly increase the chances of achieving your goals?
- How you're gonna answer these questions: talk to alumni, people in your target role

**2. Salary increase**

- What is the median salary for my target role or industry immediately after the MBA?
- What is the salary I can expect 10 years after getting an MBA in my desired role/industry?
- What is my expected salary in 10 years if I don't get an MBA?
  - Also think about 2 years out of the workforce, promotions / increases you might get during that time
- I have a cash flow model - obviously take it with a grain of salt, simplified model with a lot of assumptions
- Resources to inform your research: Target school(s)' career center reports, alumni outreach, industry research

**3. Network building (weakest typically)**

- Assess your current network. What is lacking for the next step or long-term trajectory of your career?
  - Big category: international students
- How does an MBA program help you get there?
- If you don't get an MBA, what will you do to gain a comparable network? Are you willing to do that?

Notes:
- Different factors are weighted differently depending on who you are. Career change is usually the biggest one.
- Yes there are cool things about business school other than these: self discovery, making new friends, going on trips... but I'm strongly of the opinion that those are not the reasons to get an MBA. They're good bonuses. THAT IS NOT WORTH $200K

### REASONS NOT TO GET AN MBA (long section, bullet-point format with written beats)

- You don't know what you want to do and you feel like an MBA is going to help you figure it out
  - Do not go to business school without having a clear sense of what you want to accomplish. Because let me tell you what business school is like. It's FOMO central. You're going to start hearing about people doing consulting, banking, recruiting, the offers they get and think - huh, i should do that, the signing bonus sure sounds nice... even though you wrote your admission essay on breaking into VC
  - There's so much going on that you absolutely do not have time to reflect. It's overwhelming.
- You think it's going to be an easy route to a high paying job
- You want to learn business and take classes (it's not the education let's just be REAL)
- Don't do it because you think it'll make you a better leader
- Doing it for prestige
- Because you want to start a business (SO many better ways of doing that)
- Doing it to make friends and meet more people
  - First few months - I didn't feel like I had a single real friend. Yes socializing all the time, going to parties and events, traveling... but people are trying to find their friend groups, same convos over and over
  - MBA has some of the worst people you will ever meet. "Married but available" cheating. People who screw over and scam other students. Disgusted.
  - Only after I was like, ok i guess i'm not going to make real friends here did I actually make friends
- Don't do it because you want to travel
- Don't do it because you felt like you missed out on parts of a college experience
- To take a break (it's $200K, it's not the reason)
- You got into a school already - sunk cost fallacy
- Don't go to meet a boyfriend, girlfriend (I actually met my bf in business school but I like to think i'm an exception that proves the rule)

### HOW THINGS PLAYED OUT / WAS IT WORTH IT (bullet-point riffing)

- Pivot to Product management? Could have reached that conclusion in a different way
- Salary increase - yes
- US network and soft landing? Yes accelerated way to get there, didn't NEED but it was nice
- Long term potential - this is TBD
- Unexpected benefits: boyfriend, leadership ventures, love for outdoors and hiking, led trip to Antarctica, friends

Was business school worth it? Soft YES.

## What to learn from this example

**Script format:** Not everything needs to be fully written out. The hook and vulnerable personal sections are written word-for-word. The framework and list sections are bullet points that Hannah riffs on during filming. When writing scripts for Hannah, ask which sections she wants fully scripted vs. outlined for riffing.

**Length:** This video is 17:29, longer than the default 8-12 minute target. High-stakes decision content ($200K, 2 years of your life) earns extra runtime. The 8-12 range is a default, not a ceiling.

**Hook pattern:** Disqualify the two loudest positions, then establish credibility and set the frame. "Don't believe X or Y. I graduated from Z. Here's the honest version." This works for any topic where the internet is polarized and Hannah has real experience.

**Structure:** Personal story (why I did it) > Framework (3 questions to ask yourself) > Anti-list (reasons NOT to) > Personal resolution (how it played out, soft yes). The anti-list is the longest section and the retention engine.

**Thumbnail:** Simple, clean, big text question, Hannah's face, credibility signals (school logos). The question in the thumbnail ("MBA: WORTH IT?") is the same question the viewer is asking themselves.

**Description:** Mirrors the video structure. Lists what you'll get. Ends with emotional targeting ("if you're feeling lost, burnt out, or tempted to get an MBA just because everyone else is doing it"). This is smart because it speaks to the viewer's emotional state, not just their informational need.
