# YouTube Script Writing for Hannah Zhang

Turn newsletters, raw ideas, reel scripts, or external content into YouTube scripts that sound like Hannah explaining something to a smart friend on camera. Not like an essay being read aloud. Not like a polished voiceover. Like a person talking.

## The #1 rule

**The script must sound like Hannah's transcript, not like Hannah's newsletter.**

Hannah's newsletter voice is written prose. Her YouTube voice is spoken. These are different. When you write a YouTube script, you are writing speech, not text. Every sentence should pass the test: "Would Hannah say this out loud, exactly like this, while looking into a camera?"

Read `references/example_status_income_video.md` before writing any script. It contains a cleaned transcript of how Hannah actually delivered a video, annotated with 10 specific lessons about her on-camera voice. The gap between the scripted version (v2) and how she actually talked is the gap this skill exists to close.

## Before writing anything

Read these files in order. They are the foundation for every script:

1. `references/example_status_income_video.md` - How Hannah actually talks on camera. This is the voice target. Read the annotations and the comparison table at the bottom.
2. `references/youtube_strategy.md` - Hook techniques, script structure, retention, differentiation, and lessons from previous scripts.
3. `references/repurposing_guide.md` - How to adapt each source type (newsletter, raw idea, reel, external article) into a YouTube script. Read the section that matches the input type.
4. `references/example_mba_video.md` - Hannah's best-performing YouTube video with full script, thumbnail concept, and annotations. Shows the mix of written beats + bullet points for riffing.
5. The newsletter skill's `references/voice_samples.md` - Hannah's authentic writing voice. Useful for understanding her sensibility, but remember: YouTube voice is looser than newsletter voice.
6. The newsletter skill's `references/ai_tells_to_avoid.md` - Every AI pattern that must be eliminated from the final script.

## Workflow

### Step 0: Title and thumbnail first

Do NOT write a script before locking in the title and thumbnail concept. Paddy Galloway's research shows the idea, title, and thumbnail set the ceiling for a video's performance. A great script with a bad title gets no clicks.

Present Hannah with:
- 3-5 title options (see Title guidelines below)
- 1-2 thumbnail concepts (see Thumbnail guidelines below)

Get her pick before writing a single line of script. The title and thumbnail inform the hook, which informs everything else.

### Step 1: Identify the input type

Hannah's input will be one of these:

| Input type | What it looks like | Which reference section to read |
|---|---|---|
| Newsletter | A published or draft Nonlinear News piece | Repurposing guide, section 1 |
| Raw idea / braindump | Bullet points, a thesis, a half-formed observation | Repurposing guide, section 2 |
| Reel script | A finalized short-form video script | Repurposing guide, section 3 |
| External article / trend | Something she wants to react to or comment on | Repurposing guide, section 4 |
| Interview / conversation | A transcript or conversation she wants to turn into video | Repurposing guide, section 5 |

Read the input carefully. Identify: the core thesis, any specific stories or data points, which of Hannah's content pillars it maps to, and whether there's enough material to write a strong 8-12 minute script.

### Step 2: Ask questions if the input is thin

This step matters. A thin input produces a generic script. Push back and ask rather than filling gaps with filler.

**Ask if:**
- The input is a vague topic with no personal angle or data
- There's no specific story, data point, or personal experience
- The input could go multiple directions and you're not sure which throughline she wants
- You don't know which of her experiences connects to this topic
- There's no clear contrarian angle or framework

**Questions to pick from (don't dump all of them):**
- "What's the personal story or observation behind this? Your best videos will have one."
- "Who specifically is this for, someone still in corporate, someone who just left, someone building?"
- "Is there a specific number, data point, or recent article that anchors this?"
- "What's the ONE thing you want the viewer to walk away thinking or doing differently?"
- "Is there a framework or mental model here? Your best content gives people vocabulary for things they already feel."
- "What's the contrarian angle? What do most people get wrong about this topic?"

**Don't ask if:**
- The input is a full newsletter (plenty of material)
- The input already has a personal story, data, and clear thesis
- Hannah has explicitly said what she wants

### Step 3: Find the throughline

Before writing a single word of script, identify:

**The throughline:** One sentence that captures what this video is about. Not a topic, a specific argument. "The status-income matrix exists, the gap is growing, and the low status high income quadrant is the most underrated opportunity for high achievers" is a throughline. "Status and wealth" is a topic.

**The grand payoff:** The one thing the viewer is waiting for by the end. Every section should point toward this.

**The hook territory:** What is the viewer's version of this problem? Not Hannah's experience, the viewer's pain point.

If the source material has multiple strong throughlines (common with newsletters), tell Hannah: "This could be 2-3 videos. Here's how I'd split them: [brief description of each]. Which one should we start with?"

### Step 4: Write the script using the 4-hat method

**Pass 1 - Artist:** Dump all the relevant material from the source. Personal stories, data points, quotes, examples, arguments. Don't structure yet. Just collect.

**Pass 2 - Architect:** Map the structure (see Section Structure below).

**Pass 3 - Writer:** Write it to be SPOKEN, not read. This is the most critical pass. See "How Hannah actually talks on camera" below for the specific voice targets.

**Pass 4 - Wizard:** Check retention. Where will viewers drop off? See Retention Checklist below.

## Section structure for an 8-12 minute video

### Section 1: Hook [0:00-0:15]

A statement or tension that stops the scroll. Must be about the VIEWER's problem, not about Hannah. Must work for a stranger.

**What works for Hannah:**
- Direct statement to the viewer: "If you are a high achieving person, being low status but high income is one of the most underrated ways to get rich in 2026."
- Contrarian observation: "The highest achieving people I know are stuck in careers that look impressive but aren't making them wealthy."
- Disqualifying the two loudest camps: "Don't believe anyone on the internet who says 'an MBA is definitely worth it' or 'you should never get an MBA.'"

**What does NOT work:**
- Personal story as hook ("I was at a wedding this weekend...")
- Rhetorical questions ("Have you ever wondered why...")
- Hype energy ("In this video I'm going to BLOW YOUR MIND")
- Vague promise ("This changed everything")

### Section 2: Roadmap [0:15-0:45]

**This section is mandatory.** Hannah roadmaps every video after the hook. She tells viewers what the video will cover in a casual, natural way. This is NOT signposting. It's a preview that gives the viewer reasons to stay.

Example from the status-income video:
> "I wanted to dive deeper here and go over what this matrix is and what each quadrant means. Why status has historically been a good proxy for wealth but how it's been breaking down in the last few decades. Why a lot of high achieving people feel stuck in high income, high status jobs. And finally, why low status high income is an emerging opportunity. I'm going to wrap up by giving you five specific ways to thrive in the low status high income bucket."

Example from the MBA video:
> "First I'm going to tell you a little bit about my story and why I decided to get an MBA, because everyone's situation is going to be different. I'm also going to talk about reasons NOT to get an MBA, this is a long list and you might feel attacked... caveats and disclaimers, and how things played out for me and whether I think it was personally worth it."

The roadmap should:
- List 3-5 things the video will cover
- Use natural connective language ("and finally," "I'm going to wrap up by")
- Hint at the most interesting part to create forward pull
- Take no more than 30 seconds

### Section 3: Framework / Setup [0:45-3:00]

Introduce the framework, concept, or vocabulary. Walk through it step by step. Do NOT rush this.

**Critical lesson from the status-income transcript:** Hannah walks through each quadrant of the matrix one at a time, giving examples for each. The v2 script tried to combine them for efficiency. On camera, you need to give the viewer time to absorb the framework before the argument builds.

When introducing a framework:
- Name it clearly ("It's called the status income matrix of work")
- Define the axes or dimensions simply ("Basically, how much you earn versus how much society respects you")
- Walk through each piece one at a time with examples
- Use "first," "then," "and lastly" as natural connectors
- Don't editorialize yet, just lay out the pieces

End this section with an open loop. Something that makes the viewer need to keep watching to understand WHY. Example: "To understand how they got created and why the gap between status and income widened, we have to first start with why we instinctively equate status with wealth."

### Section 4: Body / Argument [3:00-7:30]

Build the argument with 3-4 nested setup-tension-resolution loops. Each section resolves one question while opening the next.

**Pacing:** Alternate between calm explanation (15-25 seconds per idea) and burst sequences (rapid-fire examples or data). The NY Mag salary comparisons in the status-income video are a burst sequence.

**Sources and quotes:** Brief paraphrase in Hannah's own words. Attribute cleanly: "Naval Ravikant published his famous tweetstorm, How to Get Rich, where he dives into this. Basically he says..." Then one or two sentences of paraphrase. Not a block quote.

**Transitions between body sections:** Dead simple. Not crafted, not clever. Just functional:
- "But that's not always the case, and the breakdown actually started with..."
- "But this still does not explain low status high income work. The internet does."
- "So what does this mean for a lot of high achieving people?"

**Return to the framework:** Reference the core framework visually or verbally every 2-3 minutes. In the status-income video, Hannah keeps coming back to the quadrants. This re-orients the viewer and reinforces the structure.

### Section 5: Personal proof [~7:00-8:00]

Hannah's own experience as evidence. NOT a friend's story, not a secondhand anecdote. Her own career move.

Example:
> "And now I am low status, high income, working across startups in the creator economy where I have multiple streams of income. I quit to join a startup and I also create content. This is not considered a prestigious career path from the world of bankers and MBAs that I come from. But it's helped me cultivate multiple streams of income that have uncapped upside compared to those corporate jobs."

This section should:
- Be specific about her own situation (not vague or generic)
- Drop credentials naturally where they serve the argument
- Acknowledge that she's made the move she's recommending
- Include social proof from her peer group when available ("so many of my classmates are telling me they're burnt out")

### Section 6: Tactical takeaways [8:00-9:30]

**Do NOT cut this section for elegance.** The v2 script cut the five principles. Hannah filmed them because they're the actionable content that keeps viewers watching through the end and makes them feel like the video was worth their time.

Format: Numbered list, 2-3 sentences per point. Enough structure that Hannah can riff, enough specificity that each point stands on its own.

Example:
> "Number one, you need to build and monetize a very specific skill. This quadrant is less about your degree or your pedigree and it's more about what you can uniquely do. The goal is to find a skill that is so specific or a combination of skills that no one else can compete with you on."

Not every video needs a numbered list. But if the source material has actionable takeaways, include them. This is what turns a "interesting video" into a "I'm saving this video."

### Section 7: Close [9:30-10:15]

Two parts:

**Emotional closer:** One sharp line or quote that lands the thesis. Not a summary. Not "in conclusion." Something that makes the whole video click.
> "Naval Ravikant said it best, play the wealth game, not the status game."

**Practical closer / CTA:** Give the viewer something to DO next. Newsletter plug, related content, comments prompt.
> "And if you're interested in more specific career paths that are low status, high income, I wrote a Substack post last year on 11 career paths where high achieving people have an underrated advantage."
> "Let me know if you want me to dive deeper in this in another video."

Do NOT end with a philosophical reframe that tries to be profound. Hannah's actual closes are grounded and practical.

## How Hannah actually talks on camera

These patterns come from studying her actual transcript vs. scripted versions. Every script must hit these targets.

### Speech markers she uses naturally

Include these in scripts. They sound natural on camera. Removing them makes the script sound written:
- "Basically" as a bridge before simplifying something
- "In other words" before restating a point more clearly
- "You get the idea" after a list of examples
- "Think [examples]" to introduce quick illustrations
- "I'm going to wrap up by..." before the final section
- "And needless to say..." before something obvious
- "Let me tell you..." before a strong personal beat

### How she addresses the viewer

She uses "you" and "if you" throughout, not just in one section:
- "If you are a high achieving person..."
- "If you haven't read it..."
- "If you are willing to go from high status to low status..."

Scripts should address the viewer in EVERY section, not just the "for you" pivot.

### How she handles credentials

She drops them naturally when they serve the argument:
- "I have two Ivy League degrees, worked in investment banking and tech."
- "I graduated from business school two years ago."
- "This is not considered a prestigious career path from the world of bankers and MBAs that I come from."

Never as a flex. Always as context for the point she's making.

### How she transitions

Dead simple. Functional. Not clever:
- "But that's not always the case."
- "But this still does not explain [X]. [Y] does."
- "So what does this mean for..."
- "And the reason for this is..."
- "So [conclusion stated plainly]."

Do NOT write crafted transitions like "Then the internet came along and broke that model." Write "The internet gave rise to low status high income work." Direct. Plain.

### How she walks through lists and frameworks

One item at a time, with examples for each:
- "First over here, you have [X]. This is [description]. [2-3 examples]."
- "Then you have [Y]. This is [description]. Think [examples]."
- "And lastly, [Z]. This is where it gets interesting because..."

Don't rush through frameworks. Each piece needs room to land.

### Level of detail in scripts

The v2 script was underwritten. Hannah's actual delivery is more detailed and more fleshed out than what was on paper. Compare:

**v2 script (too thin):**
> "Two quadrants make sense. Low status, low income: cashiers, fast food workers, a lot of blue collar jobs. High status, high income: doctors, lawyers, bankers, engineers."

**How Hannah actually delivered it (right level of detail):**
> "First over here, you have low income low status. This one's pretty self-explanatory. It's a lot of blue collar work, fast food workers, taxi drivers, plumbers. Then you have low income high status. This is work that's well respected but not well paid. Think teachers, nurses, artists, creatives, non-profit workers, journalists. Then in the upper right, you have the one everyone wants to be, high income high status. Doctors, engineers, lawyers, bankers, you get the idea. Of course, because everyone wants to be this, this quadrant attracts a lot of high achieving smart and ambitious people."

The actual version takes each piece separately, gives it a sentence of context, and adds commentary. This is the level of detail scripts need.

## Script format: written vs. bullet points

Not every section of a script needs to be written word-for-word. Hannah's best videos use a mix:

**Fully write out:** The hook, the roadmap, vulnerable personal admissions, transitions between major sections, the emotional close. These are moments where exact word choice and pacing matter.

**Bullet points for riffing:** Framework walkthroughs (when she knows the material cold), numbered list sections (principles, reasons, tips), examples within a section. Hannah is naturally good at expanding on bullets during filming. Over-scripting these sections flattens her energy.

**When to ask Hannah:** If you're not sure which sections she wants scripted vs. outlined, ask. Some videos are mostly scripted (the status-income matrix video). Some are mostly bullets with a few written beats (the MBA video). It depends on the topic and how comfortable she is with the material.

See `references/example_mba_video.md` for how this mix works in practice.
See `references/example_status_income_video.md` for the level of detail and voice target.

## Title guidelines

Present titles BEFORE writing the script. The title informs the hook.

### Title research findings (2026)

- Keep titles between 40-65 characters. Most compelling words in the first 40 characters (mobile truncation point).
- Title and thumbnail should complement, not duplicate. Each communicates something the other doesn't.
- "Why" outperforms "How to" for educational content.
- Numbers and credibility signals increase CTR 20-30%.
- One power word max, used meaningfully. Avoid "insane," "unbelievable," "shocking."
- YouTube now evaluates "Quality CTR," meaning what happens in the 30 seconds after the click. Misleading titles get suppressed.
- Paddy Galloway: come up with many title options, narrow to top 3.

### Title patterns that work for Hannah

**Curiosity gap:** "The Career Move Prestigious People Won't Make" (46 chars). Implies something surprising without revealing it. The viewer must click to find out.

**Personal/contrarian:** "Why I Stopped Chasing Prestige (Wharton MBA)" (46 chars). Personal, implies a story with a resolution. Credential in parentheses.

**Warning/contrast:** "Your Prestigious Career Is a Trap" (34 chars). Short, blunt, fear-based. Speaks to the viewer's situation.

**Framework:** "The 2x2 Matrix That Explains Why Smart People Stay Broke" (56 chars). Names the framework. "Smart people stay broke" is provocative for her audience.

**Pre-decision:** "Watch this before you get an MBA" (32 chars). Positions the video as essential viewing before a big decision.

**What to avoid in titles:**
- Hype language ("SHOCKING," "INSANE," "YOU WON'T BELIEVE")
- Rhetorical questions as titles
- Titles that could work for any generic career channel
- Titles longer than 65 characters

### Title formulas ranked by fit for Hannah's niche

1. **Curiosity gap** - strongest for framework-driven content
2. **Personal/contrarian** - strongest for personal story content
3. **Warning/contrast** - strongest for "wake up" content aimed at high achievers
4. **Pre-decision** - strongest for high-stakes decision content (MBA, quitting, pivoting)

## Thumbnail guidelines

Present thumbnail concepts alongside titles. They work as a pair.

### Thumbnail concept format

When presenting a thumbnail concept, describe:

1. **Hannah's pose and expression** - what she's doing, what emotion she's conveying
2. **Text overlay** - the exact words on the thumbnail (2-5 words, large and readable)
3. **Supporting elements** - any logos, numbers, graphics, or visual props
4. **Background** - what's behind her
5. **Why it works** - one sentence on why this thumbnail would make someone click, and how it complements the title

### What works for Hannah's thumbnails

- Hannah's face with a clear expression (thinking, direct eye contact, slight smirk)
- Bold, short text (2-5 words max). The text should be the viewer's question or the core tension.
- One credibility signal if relevant (school logos, salary numbers, company names)
- Clean background, high contrast
- A framework graphic if the video introduces one (like the status-income matrix)

### What to avoid

- Cluttered thumbnails with too many elements
- Text that repeats the title exactly (complement, don't duplicate)
- Clickbait expressions that don't match Hannah's brand
- Generic stock-style imagery

### Examples from Hannah's videos

**MBA video:** Hannah in white t-shirt, chin on hand (thinking pose), direct eye contact. Text: "MBA: WORTH IT?" Three school crests (Harvard, Stanford, Wharton) stacked on right. Campus background. Works because: viewer's question on screen, school logos signal credibility.

**Status-income video:** Hannah with slight smirk, arms crossed, direct eye contact. Text: "LOW STATUS HIGH INCOME?" The 2x2 matrix graphic with bottom-right quadrant highlighted. Works because: the matrix is a visual framework people haven't seen, the text asks the viewer's question, Hannah's expression signals insider knowledge.

## Description writing

Every script deliverable includes a description draft. The description is not an afterthought. It's a discovery tool (YouTube's algorithm reads it for ranking), a preview (the first 150 characters show before "Show more"), and a retention aid (timestamps let impatient viewers jump to the part they care about instead of leaving).

### Description structure

**Line 1-2: Above-the-fold hook (150 characters max)**

This is the only part most viewers see. It must do two things: hook with the tension or contrarian premise, and include the primary keyword naturally. This is NOT a summary. It's a reason to watch.

Good: "If you're a high achieving person wondering why your prestigious career isn't making you wealthy, this is the framework that explains it."
Bad: "Hey guys, in this video I talk about the status-income matrix and why it matters."

The first version hooks with the viewer's problem and includes the keyword naturally. The second is a generic summary that gives the viewer no reason to click "Show more" or watch.

**Lines 3-6: What the video covers**

A bulleted list of 4-7 things the video covers. Each bullet should create curiosity, not just inform. The viewer should think "I want to know that" for each one.

Good:
- What the status-income matrix is and what each quadrant means
- Why we're wired to equate status with wealth (Naval Ravikant's explanation)
- How the internet flipped the relationship between status and income (with real salary data from NY Mag)
- 5 principles for thriving in the low status, high income bucket

Bad:
- Intro to the matrix
- History of status
- The internet section
- Tips

The good version gives specific, interesting previews. The bad version is a table of contents with no pull.

**Lines 7-8: Emotional targeting**

One sentence describing who this video is for, using their feelings and situation, not demographics. This line speaks directly to the viewer's internal state and makes them feel seen.

Example: "If you went to a good school, work at a company people recognize, and you're starting to wonder whether prestige is actually building wealth or just keeping you on a treadmill, this one's for you."

This works because it names the specific situation (good school, brand-name company) and the specific feeling (wondering if prestige = wealth). It's Hannah talking to her audience, not a marketing team writing copy.

**Timestamps section**

Formatted as chapters. YouTube indexes these as "Key Moments" in Google search results, which drives additional discovery. Minimum 3 chapters. Always start at 0:00. Use keywords in chapter titles where natural.

Format:
```
0:00 - Why low status high income is underrated
0:45 - The status-income matrix explained
3:00 - Why we equate status with wealth
```

Don't over-segment. 5-8 chapters for a 10-minute video is the sweet spot. Chapter titles should be descriptive enough that a viewer can jump to the section they care about.

**Links section**

3-7 links total. Internal links to Hannah's other videos boost session watch time (YouTube rewards this). Include:
- Newsletter link (always)
- 1-2 related videos or Substack posts that go deeper on a subtopic mentioned in the video
- Social handles (Instagram, LinkedIn)

Keep it clean. No link dump. Each link should feel intentional.

**Hashtags**

3-5 specific hashtags at the bottom. More than 15 and YouTube ignores all of them. Use topic-specific tags, not generic ones.

Good: #statusandwealth #careerstrategy #nonlinearcareer
Bad: #motivation #success #viral #fyp #trending

## Voice check (Step 5)

Compare the script to the status-income transcript (the voice target). Ask:

1. Does every sentence sound like something Hannah would say on camera, exactly as written?
2. Is she using natural speech markers ("basically," "in other words," "you get the idea")?
3. Are transitions dead simple and functional, not crafted or cinematic?
4. Is she addressing the viewer throughout ("if you are," "a lot of you"), not just in one section?
5. Is she walking through frameworks step by step, giving each piece room to land?
6. Are credentials dropped naturally where they serve the argument?
7. Is the personal proof section about HER experience, not a secondhand anecdote?
8. Is there a roadmap after the hook?
9. Is there a tactical takeaways section?
10. Does the close have both an emotional line AND a practical plug?

### The critical comparison test

Read this sentence from the v2 script:
> "The high status, low income job is what you get when institutions give people status but keep the wealth for themselves."

Now read how Hannah actually said it:
> "So high status low income work is a product of institutions giving people status but keeping the wealth for themselves."

The second version starts with "So" (natural bridge), uses "work" instead of "job" (more casual), and drops the constructed "what you get when" framing. These differences matter. The first sounds written. The second sounds spoken.

Every line in the script should sound like the second version.

## Humanizer pass (Step 6)

Run the humanizer skill on the finished script. Then do a manual scan for these patterns that are specific to YouTube scripts:

**YouTube-specific AI tells to catch:**
- Signposting: "In this video I'm going to show you," "Let me explain," "Here's the thing"
- Announcement headers: "Now let's talk about," "The next thing is"
- Fake casual: "You know what's wild," "Here's the crazy part"
- Manufactured energy: "This is SO important," "You NEED to hear this"
- Tidy summaries: "So to recap," "The three things to remember are"
- Crafted transitions: "Then the internet came along and broke that model" (too cinematic)
- "Actually" (banned word for Hannah)
- "Quietly" (banned word)
- Em dashes (replace with commas, periods, or parentheses)
- "Not X, but Y" as a standalone construction
- Rhetorical questions of any kind
- Sentences that sound like they belong in a written essay, not spoken aloud

**The ultimate test:** Read the entire script out loud. If any line sounds like a script instead of a person talking, rewrite it.

## Output format

The script should be a clean markdown document with:
- Title and metadata at the top (target length, format, source)
- Sections labeled with timestamps
- Script text as regular paragraphs (not in tables)
- Bracketed production cues only where necessary: [Show the matrix graphic on screen] or [Cut to NY Mag salary screenshots]
- No visual notes column
- No filming instructions
- No bold emphasis on words (this isn't a teleprompter)

### What to include with every script delivery

1. The full script (written beats + bullet-point sections as appropriate)
2. Estimated runtime
3. One sentence on what the throughline is
4. Title (locked in during Step 0)
5. Thumbnail concept (locked in during Step 0)
6. Description draft (follow the structure in the "Description writing" section: above-the-fold hook, bullet list, emotional targeting, timestamps, links, hashtags)
7. If repurposed: what was cut from the source and why (so Hannah knows what could become separate videos)

## Retention checklist (Wizard pass)

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Writing an essay, not speech.** The biggest recurring problem. "The high status, low income job is what you get when institutions give people status but keep the wealth for themselves" is essay writing. "So high status low income work is a product of institutions giving people status but keeping the wealth for themselves" is speech. Start sentences with "So," "And," "But," "Basically." Use contractions. Use "like" and "kind of." Write speech.

**Mistake 2: Rushing frameworks.** Walk through each piece one at a time with examples. Don't combine quadrants or steps for efficiency. On camera, the viewer needs time to absorb.

**Mistake 3: Cutting tactical content for elegance.** If the source material has principles, steps, or actionable takeaways, include them. They're the value. Cutting them makes the video feel like an interesting idea with no payoff.

**Mistake 4: Secondhand personal proof.** "A friend at a wedding told me..." is weak. "I quit to join a startup and I also create content. This is not considered a prestigious career path from the world of bankers and MBAs that I come from" is strong. Hannah's own story IS the proof.

**Mistake 5: Philosophical close instead of practical close.** Don't end with a profound reframe. End with an emotional one-liner (the Naval quote) + a practical next step (Substack plug, comments ask, related video tease).

**Mistake 6: Newsletter opening as YouTube hook.** Newsletter readers know Hannah. YouTube viewers don't. Never open with a personal story unless it directly names a tension the viewer feels.

**Mistake 7: Saving "you" language for one section.** Address the viewer throughout. "If you are a high achieving person" in the hook. "If you haven't read it" in the body. "If you are willing to go from high status to low status" in the pivot. Not just in one "for you" section.

**Mistake 8: Skipping the roadmap.** Hannah roadmaps every video after the hook. It's not optional. It sets expectations and gives the viewer reasons to stay.

**Mistake 9: AI slop surviving the humanizer.** Run the humanizer, then do a manual pass. Read every line out loud. YouTube is more exposed than text because the viewer hears every word.

**Mistake 10: Underwriting the script.** The script should be detailed enough that Hannah could read it verbatim and it would sound natural. Don't write thin outlines and expect her to fill in the gaps. The written sections should be at the level of detail shown in the status-income transcript, not the v2 script.
