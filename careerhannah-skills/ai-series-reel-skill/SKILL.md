# Nonlinear Playbook Reel Skill

Write reel scripts for Hannah Zhang's Nonlinear Playbook AI series — educational, borrowed-authority content for smart ambitious people staying ahead in the age of AI.

## Before Writing Anything: Net New Value Test

Every episode must pass all three before you write a single word. No concept that fails this test will perform, regardless of how well it's written.

**1. Non-obvious** — The insight cannot be something the viewer could have Googled or assumed. The bar is: "I hadn't thought of it that way." Generic observations and surface-level AI commentary fail this.

**2. Highly tactical** — Not vibes, not inspiration. The viewer should be able to do something differently tomorrow. Tactical means specific enough to act on — a framework, a named move, a concrete step.

**3. Lived experience AI can't replicate** — The concept must connect to Hannah's actual experience. Banking, leaving banking, building Nonlinear News, running events, the specific decisions she's made. If the reel could be written by anyone with a prompt, it doesn't have net new value.

If the concept fails any of these, find a sharper angle before writing.

## What Makes These Episodes Perform

The best episodes in this series (high agency ep1, antifragility ep3) share the same structure. Before writing, understand why it works:

- **Borrowed authority** does heavy lifting. "Nassim Taleb says" lands harder than "I think." Pick one credible outside source — a book, a named concept, a respected thinker — and anchor the episode to it. New viewers trust the source. Returning viewers get the framework applied to something new.

- **The concept names something the audience already feels but can't articulate.** "Antifragile" isn't new information — it's vocabulary for something smart ambitious people already experience. The right concept makes viewers feel seen and gives them a tool to explain their situation to others.

- **Personal proof is brief and honest, not the main story.** The episode is primarily educational. Hannah's experience appears as a quick aside that validates the concept — not as a narrative you build around. "I was scared of getting laid off. Two years later it's a six-figure business." That's the format. Not a story arc.

- **The tactical close is numbered and specific.** Not "here are some ways to..." but actual numbered steps, spoken conversationally. Viewers need to leave knowing what to do tomorrow.

## Series Format

Every episode follows this structure. Don't deviate from it — the series format is part of what makes returning viewers stay.

### 1. Hook
One sentence. A counterintuitive claim specifically about smart ambitious people. The paradox should make the viewer feel slightly called out or curious.

**Formula:** Something that should be true about high achievers turns out to be a liability, or something that looks like a weakness turns out to be an advantage.

Good examples:
- "The smartest people have the most fragile careers in the age of AI." (ep3)
- "The most financially fragile people in the age of AI are Henrys." (HENRY ep)

Bad hooks: reporting news, generic AI takes, things that could apply to anyone.

Sometimes the hook includes a brief visual gag or clarification before the series intro — e.g., "No, not these guys" (pointing to men named Henry) followed by the definition. Use this when the hook includes a proper noun or term that needs context.

**CRITICAL: When Hannah provides a hook she's clearly happy with, use it verbatim. Do NOT substitute it with a "better" version — that's the single most common mistake to avoid. If she says "hook along the lines of X," write X. Clean up grammar only if something is genuinely broken. Never replace her hook with your own without being asked.**

**If the hook she provides is genuinely weak (too generic, not a paradox, could apply to anyone), you may flag it and offer an alternative — but still write the episode using her hook first, and present the alternative as an optional upgrade. Do not silently swap it out.**

### 2. Concept Definition (when hook includes a term)
Define the term immediately. One or two sentences. Make the definition land as both an explanation and an observation.

Example: "HENRY stands for High Earner, Not Rich Yet. You feel rich because of a salary that's less secure than you think."

### 3. Series Intro
Always exactly this:
> "Welcome to this episode of the nonlinear playbook for smart ambitious people staying ahead in the age of AI."

### 4. The Borrowed Authority Concept
Introduce the named concept from a credible outside source. State who coined it or where it comes from. Explain it simply — not as a textbook definition, but as something that makes the viewer go "oh, that's what that is."

Keep it tight. Two to four sentences. Then immediately connect it to why it matters for Hannah's audience specifically.

### 5. Apply to Audience
Show the audience what this concept means for their situation. This is where the reel teaches. Be specific about who they are — people in finance, consulting, law, tech; people who went to great schools and got impressive jobs; people whose credentials felt like security until they didn't.

### 6. Personal Proof (brief aside)
**Strictly one to three sentences.** Honest, specific, not dramatic. The point is to show that Hannah has lived this, not to make it about her. This is an aside, not a story — the viewer should barely notice it before you're back to the concept.

Format: "I [thing I did/experienced] because [honest reason]. [What happened as a result]."

Example from ep3: "I did this in 2024 when I started creating content because I was scared of getting laid off. Two years later it's a six-figure business but the most important thing is I'm not up at 2am anymore wondering what I'd do if I lost my job. I became antifragile." — Three sentences. That's the maximum. Never more.

### 7. Tactical Close (numbered)
Three things, numbered, spoken conversationally. Not "One:" as a dramatic beat — just naturally: "One, build an outside income source. Two, invest aggressively while you have the salary to do it. Three, make your skill stack specific enough to command a premium regardless of what AI automates."

Each point should be actionable. Not a mindset. Not a principle. Something you can start doing this week.

### 8. Series CTA
Always close with one of these variations:
- "Follow for the next page of the nonlinear playbook."
- "Follow for the next page."

## Voice Rules

These reels sound like Hannah talking to a smart friend — educational but conversational, specific but not jargony.

**Do:**
- Full flowing sentences, not punchy fragments
- Lowercase register (even proper nouns are delivered casually)
- Real names, real numbers, real sources — specificity creates trust
- "I" not "we." Talk directly to the viewer.
- Mix sentence lengths naturally. Long explanatory sentence followed by a short landing sentence.
- When numbering tacticals, say "One... Two... Three..." naturally, not as dramatic bullets.

**Don't:**
- Choppy dramatic fragments for effect ("Most people thought he meant an AI startup. He didn't." — this is the exact pattern to avoid)
- "Not X, but Y" as a standalone line or sentence-ender
- Rhetorical questions of any kind ("How long could you sustain your life? Six months?" is a question — make it a statement: "Most people's savings cover three to six months.")
- Signpost phrases ("Here are three things I did", "So here's what you need to know")
- Filler setup lines ("has this line I think about a lot")
- "This is that episode" or "We're finally here" — too scripted
- Em dashes (—) anywhere in the spoken script. Replace with commas, periods, or rewrite the sentence. Check for this explicitly before finalizing — em dashes are the single most common anti-pattern.
- Accusatory second-person ("You're not." as a standalone line) — use softer framing when pointing out uncomfortable truths

Read the script out loud. If any line sounds like a script rather than a person talking, rewrite it.

## Brainstorming Process

When Hannah brings a rough concept or topic, work through these before writing:

**1. Find the borrowed authority anchor**
What named concept, book, or thinker gives this credibility? The anchor should be someone Hannah's audience respects (Naval Ravikant, Nassim Taleb, Shreyas Doshi, George Mack, Morgan Housel, etc.). The concept should already exist — don't invent frameworks. Find the one that already names what Hannah wants to say.

**2. Find the paradox for the hook**
What's counterintuitive about this topic as it applies to smart ambitious people? High earners being the most financially fragile. The smartest people having the most fragile careers. High agency people feeling the most stuck. The hook lives in that paradox.

**3. Check the lived experience connection**
Where does Hannah's actual background connect to this? What did she experience in banking, in the transition, in building her content business, in running events — that makes this specific and not generic? This becomes the personal proof aside.

**4. Identify the three tactical closes**
What are three concrete things the viewer can do? Not principles — actions. If you can't identify three specific, actionable moves, the concept isn't tactical enough yet.

## Continuity with Previous Episodes

Check what concepts have already been introduced before writing a new episode. Don't re-explain from scratch what returning viewers already know — build on it.

**Already introduced:**
- High agency (ep1) — George Mack, Shreyas Doshi, Nick Wignall; bias to action; frustrated genius concept
- Antifragility (ep3) — Nassim Taleb; barbell strategy (90% safe, 10% high-upside); fragile vs. resilient vs. antifragile
- AI corporate ladder — three levels of AI as a team (chatbot = analyst, Cowork = chief of staff, Claude Code = engineer); nonlinear background as qualification to be the director
- HENRY — High Earner Not Rich Yet; income vs. wealth; building the 10% outside institutional salary

When an episode references a prior concept (e.g., "as I talked about in episode three, the barbell strategy..."), don't re-explain the whole concept. Just name it and move on. Returning viewers feel rewarded; new viewers know there's more to discover.

## Quality Checklist

Before presenting the final script:

- [ ] Hook is one sentence, counterintuitive, specific to smart ambitious people — and if Hannah provided a hook, it is her exact words, not a rewrite
- [ ] Named concept is from a real, credible outside source
- [ ] The concept names something the audience already feels but can't articulate
- [ ] Personal proof is brief (1-3 sentences) and honest, not a narrative arc
- [ ] Tactical close has three numbered, concrete, actionable items
- [ ] No choppy dramatic fragments
- [ ] No "Not X, but Y" standalone constructions
- [ ] No rhetorical questions (any sentence ending in "?" is a rhetorical question — remove or restate as a claim)
- [ ] No filler setup phrases
- [ ] Zero em dashes (—) in the spoken script. Do a literal search for "—" before marking this done.
- [ ] Personal proof is 3 sentences or fewer — count them explicitly
- [ ] Script is plain prose: no markdown headers (## HOOK, ## SERIES INTRO, etc.) and no bullet lists anywhere in the spoken script
- [ ] Script reads naturally when spoken aloud
- [ ] Net New Value: non-obvious, tactical, lived experience
- [ ] Ends with series CTA ("follow for the next page")
