# Anti-Patterns for the Nonlinear Playbook Series

Extended list of what to avoid, with specific examples from feedback on this series.

---

## Series-Specific Anti-Patterns

### 1. "This is that episode" phrasing

**Bad:** "In episode zero I said I'd show you how to build capital that doesn't depend on an institution. This is that episode."

**Why it's bad:** Too scripted. Hannah doesn't talk this way. It signals a written script rather than someone talking to a friend.

**Fix:** Just start the episode. The hook and series intro do enough framing work. Don't call attention to the architecture.

---

### 2. "Has this line I think about a lot"

**Bad:** "[Thinker] has this line I think about a lot: [quote]"

**Why it's bad:** Filler setup. Adds zero value. The quote stands on its own.

**Fix:** Attribute the thinker briefly, then give the concept. "Nassim Taleb calls this antifragility." Not "Nassim Taleb has this concept I think about a lot."

---

### 3. Rhetorical questions as teaching devices

**Bad:** "How long could you actually sustain your life? Six months? Three?"

**Why it's bad:** AI slop that says nothing. Sounds like a self-help podcast. Doesn't teach anything. Makes the viewer feel lectured.

**Fix:** Make it a statement. "Most people's savings cover three to six months — that's not wealth, it's a delay."

---

### 4. Treadmill/hamster wheel metaphors

**Bad:** "You're on a very expensive treadmill."

**Why it's bad:** Overused metaphor that shows up in every personal finance reel ever made. Generic.

**Fix:** Be specific about what the actual problem is. What does being on a treadmill actually mean in concrete terms for a HENRY?

---

### 5. The standalone accusation

**Bad:** "You're not." (as its own line after "You feel rich...")

**Why it's bad:** Too harsh. Accusatory framing makes the viewer defensive rather than curious. Hannah's tone points out uncomfortable truths but with empathy.

**Fix:** Fold the contradiction into the same sentence. "You feel rich because of a salary that's less secure than you think." One sentence, both things.

---

### 6. Repeating frameworks without a thread

**Bad:** Scripts that hit multiple frameworks (HENRY, barbell, skill stack) without a clear through-line connecting them.

**Why it's bad:** Feels like a listicle. No pull. Viewer can check out after the first point because there's no unresolved tension keeping them in.

**Fix:** The borrowed authority concept should be the spine. Everything else hangs off it. Ask: what's the one insight the viewer is waiting to fully understand? Build toward that.

---

### 7. "Smart ambitious people are [X]"

**Bad:** "Smart ambitious people are great at earning money and terrible at owning anything."

**Why it's bad:** Too broad to be a hook. "Smart ambitious people" with a generic observation reads like a LinkedIn thought leader. The hook needs a specific paradox, not a general observation.

**Fix:** Name the specific group more precisely. Not "smart ambitious people" — "Henrys," "people who went to great schools and got impressive jobs in finance or tech," etc. The hook is stronger when it calls out a specific situation.

---

### 8. Choppy dramatic fragments (general anti-pattern)

**Bad:** "Most people thought he meant an AI startup. He didn't."

**Why it's bad:** A writing trick that AI overuses. Creates false drama and staccato rhythm that doesn't match Hannah's voice. She uses short sentences sometimes but not as a constant rhetorical device.

**Fix:** Connect the ideas naturally. "Most people thought he meant an AI startup, but he didn't" reads like a person talking. Two sentences reads like a copywriter.

---

### 9. "Not X, but Y" as a standalone construction

**Bad:** "Not because they chose it, but because the linear path stopped existing."

**Why it's bad:** TED talk punchline energy. Artificial drama. Dead AI tell.

**Fix:** Fold it into a natural sentence. "Everyone will have a nonlinear career, even if they didn't choose one, because the linear path stops existing."

---

### 10. Building around the personal story instead of the concept

**Bad:** Scripts that open with Hannah's banking story and build toward the concept.

**Why it's bad:** The series is primarily educational. Viewers are here for the framework, not Hannah's autobiography. Personal proof validates — it's not the main event.

**Fix:** Concept first, personal aside second. The aside should be 1-3 sentences, honest, and specific. "I started creating content two years ago because I was scared of getting laid off. That's now a six-figure business." That's the format.

---

### 11. Re-explaining concepts from previous episodes

**Bad:** "Nassim Taleb's concept of antifragility means that fragile things break under stress, resilient things bounce back, but antifragile things actually get stronger..." (in an episode that already assumes the viewer knows ep3)

**Why it's bad:** Treats returning viewers like they have no memory. Kills pacing.

**Fix:** Reference the prior concept by name and move on. "Using the barbell strategy I talked about in episode three" is enough. New viewers know there's more to discover. Returning viewers feel rewarded.

---

### 12. Signpost phrases

**Bad:** "Here are three things I did." / "So here's what you need to know." / "Let me break this down."

**Why it's bad:** Announces the structure instead of just doing it. Sounds like a how-to video, not a smart person sharing insight.

**Fix:** Just say the things. "One, build an outside income source." No intro needed.

---

### 13. Generic borrowed authority

**Bad:** Using a financial influencer or content creator as the source instead of a credentialed expert or named concept from a published work.

**Why it's bad:** Hannah's audience respects expertise. A thinker's credibility needs to be earned — and that credibility transfers to the episode. Content creators without credentials don't have the same weight as authors, economists, or researchers.

**Fix:** Anchor to named concepts from books, credentialed researchers, respected thinkers (Naval Ravikant, Nassim Taleb, Shreyas Doshi, George Mack, Morgan Housel). The concept should already exist — don't invent frameworks.

---

## Quick Reference: Words and Phrases to Never Use

- "This is that episode"
- "Has this line I think about a lot"
- "The brutal truth is"
- "And here's the thing"
- "Hit different"
- "Future-proof" (banned in all Hannah content)
- "The real question is"
- "Sound familiar?"
- "Actually" (banned in YouTube scripts, avoid here too)
- Em dashes for rhythm (replace with commas or periods)

## What Good Looks Like by Comparison

From ep3 (antifragility) — the benchmark episode:

> "The concept of being antifragile comes from Nassim Taleb — he wrote this book about how everything in the world is either fragile, resilient, or antifragile. Fragile things break under stress. Resilient things survive stress and stay the same. But antifragile things actually get stronger from stress and disorder."

Notice: clean attribution, simple explanation, then immediately applied to the audience. No drama, no setup, no filler. The viewer gets the concept and can already see where it's going.
