## Episode 1: High Agency

**Hook:** Every single person who thrives in the age of AI will have this trait in common. Here's how to develop it.

**Series intro:** This is page 1 of the nonlinear career playbook for smart ambitious people to stay ahead of AI when the linear paths are falling apart.

**This trait is high agency.**

Let me read you three definitions for high agency that I love:

- "Finding a way to get what you want without waiting for conditions to be perfect or blaming your circumstances." — Shreyas Doshi
- Instead of *I need to understand this before I can try it,* thinking *I need to try this in order to understand it.* — Nick Wignall
- Clear thinking plus bias to action plus disagreeability — George Mack

Being high agency has never been more important. AI changes things so fast that most people's instinct is to bury their head in the sand, while high agency people are thinking: what can I learn, how can I pivot, what can I build?

A lot of smart ambitious people are high talent but low agency — what Shreyas Doshi calls frustrated geniuses. You're smart but feel stuck. I was there when I quit banking and had no idea what to do next. I was there before I started creating content.

If you're in this boat, the number one thing you can do to start practicing high agency is develop a bias for action because you already have the talent. The best way to do that is to be around other people with these traits — join startups, specific communities, even creating content to attract these types of people online.

## Episode 3: Antifragility

**Hook:** The smartest people have the most fragile careers in the age of AI.

**Series intro:** This is page 3 of the nonlinear career playbook for smart ambitious people to stay ahead when the linear career paths are falling apart.

The concept of being antifragile comes from Nassim Taleb — he wrote this book about how everything in the world is either fragile, resilient, or antifragile. Fragile things break under stress. Resilient things survive stress and stay the same. But antifragile things actually get stronger from stress and disorder.

Nassim says most smart ambitious people have been fragilized by corporate systems. You're a high achiever, you got the great job — but everything you built — your credibility, your network, your income — it all lives inside one institution.

If you want to become antifragile in your career, finances and life, you need what Nassim calls a barbell strategy — you put 90% in the safest possible assets and 10% in high-upside bets. In your career, an example would be to keep your job as your 90%. But start building something on the other end. Consult in your area of expertise, vibe code a tool, start a side project.

I did this in 2024 when I started creating content because I was scared of getting laid off. Two years later it's a six-figure business but the most important thing is I'm not up at 2am anymore wondering what I'd do if I lost my job. I became antifragile.

I'm going to put more tips in the caption on how to start building your 10%. Follow for the next page of the nonlinear career playbook.

## HENRY Episode (in progress)

**Hook:** The most financially fragile people in the age of AI are Henrys.

No, not these guys.

HENRY stands for High Earner, Not Rich Yet. You feel rich because of a salary that's less secure than you think.

**Series intro:** Welcome to this episode of the nonlinear playbook for smart ambitious people staying ahead in the age of AI.

If you're a Henry — and it's okay, I am too — you probably went to a great school, got a great job in finance, tech, law, or something else that looks impressive on LinkedIn.

Up until recently this felt like the best path to wealth. High paying skill, invest aggressively, let it compound.

The problem is a lot of our jobs are exactly what AI is coming for. Finance, law, middle management. Even if you're not replaced, your earning power goes down and your risk of layoff goes up.

Three things I did to become financially antifragile — borrowing that term from Nassim Taleb.

One: build an outside income source. Use the skills from your 9-5 to consult, freelance, or build something. I started creating content two years ago because I was scared of getting laid off. That's now a six-figure business.

Two: invest aggressively while you have the salary to do it. I use the barbell strategy — 90% in safe assets like my 401k, 10% in higher-risk bets including crypto. The high income is a window. Use it.

Three: make your skill stack specific enough that you command a premium regardless of what AI automates. The generalist gets compressed. The person with a rare combination doesn't.

I'll keep going on all three of these, but if you take one thing: start building in your 5-9. More income to invest, harder skill stack to replace.

## Mimetic Ambition Episode

**Hook:** If you are a conventionally successful person, you probably have mimetic ambition.

Welcome to this episode of the nonlinear playbook for smart ambitious people staying ahead in the age of AI.

Naval Ravikant, the author of How to Get Rich, calls humans "highly mimetic creatures." we copy the desires of the people around us. This is proven in studies where people rate objects as more desirable simply after seeing someone else want them.

When I was in college studying econ, everyone wanted to be in banking so I wanted to be in banking. When I was recruiting for tech, everyone wanted to work at a FAANG so I wanted that too. And it's even worse now with social media — you can see what everyone else is doing and it always looks better from the outside.

The problem with mimetic ambition is that it makes you a copy of the people around you, and skills you can copy are what AI is coming for first. The people who are hardest to replace are doing things no one else can do, because it's an extension of who they authentically are.

As Naval says, you can "escape competition through authenticity"

easy to say, but when mimetic ambition is the default, you have to go out of your way to become authentic in the age of AI. 3 ways how:

1. Curate your inputs. Mimetic desire is strongest from people closest to you. If your entire circle or feed is people in the same industry doing the same thing, you're going to want that thing. Deliberately changing who you're around changes what you want. consume and do things out of left field.

2. Ask yourself this question from Peter Thiel: what is something you believe that almost nobody else agrees with you on? It's his filter for whether you're actually thinking for yourself.

3. Build your unique skill stack. This comes from Scott Adams, the creator of Dilbert who says you don't have to be the best at any one thing but if you're in the top 25% of two or three things that rarely overlap, that combination is your moat. Scott wasn't the best artist, writer, or businessman — but the overlap is why no one else could have created Dilbert.

## What These Examples Have in Common

Reading across all three, notice:

**Length:** Each is roughly 200-280 words spoken. Not short, but not long. Every sentence earns its place.

**Pacing:** Long explanatory sentences alternate with shorter landing sentences. The numbered tacticals are each one to two sentences — concrete, not padded.

**Personal proof position:** Hannah's story appears once, briefly, after the concept is established. It validates the framework rather than driving it.

**Source handling:** Named thinkers get brief attribution ("Nassim Taleb — he wrote this book about how...") but no long quotes. One or two sentences from the source's framework, then immediately applied to the audience.

**Series continuity:** The HENRY episode references ep3's barbell strategy without re-explaining it — "borrowing that term from Nassim Taleb" is enough for returning viewers.

**Personal proof variation:** In the Mimetic Ambition episode, Hannah's personal proof is woven into the "apply to audience" section rather than appearing as a separate aside after it. ("When I was in college studying econ, everyone wanted to be in banking so I wanted to be in banking.") This works when the proof IS the illustration of the concept, not just a validation of it.

**Multiple borrowed authorities in tacticals:** The Mimetic Ambition episode uses Naval as the primary anchor but brings in Peter Thiel and Scott Adams in the numbered tacticals. This is acceptable when each authority is named briefly and the primary concept anchor stays clear throughout. Don't use more than one authority per tactical point.
