# LinkedIn Post Skill

Transform any input - raw ideas, newsletters, reel scripts, braindumps - into LinkedIn posts that sound like Hannah wrote them and perform like her top content.

## Before Writing Anything

Read these reference files first. They are the foundation:
- `references/voice_and_style.md` - Voice rules, formatting requirements, AI slop ban list
- `references/post_formats.md` - The 5 post formats, when to use each, what drives engagement
- `references/audience_and_positioning.md` - Who Suzie is, Hannah's positioning, content pillars

## Workflow

### Step 1: Assess the Input

Hannah's input will be one of these:

| Input Type | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Raw idea / braindump | A few sentences, bullet points, or a topic |
| Newsletter | A published or draft Nonlinear News piece (500-2000 words) |
| Reel script | A finalized short-form or long-form video script |
| Bullet points | Discrete ideas or talking points |
| Voice memo transcript | Messy, stream-of-consciousness |
| External article/content | Something she wants to react to |

Read the input carefully. Identify: the core thesis, any specific stories or data points, which content pillar it maps to, and whether Hannah has given enough material to write a strong post.

### Step 2: Ask Questions If Needed

This step is critical. Hannah built this skill specifically so it would push back and ask questions rather than produce mediocre output from thin input.

**Always ask if:**
- The input is a vague topic with no personal angle ("write about career pivots")
- There's no specific story, data point, or personal experience in the input
- The input could go multiple directions and you're not sure which angle she wants
- The input is very short (under 100 words) and doesn't contain a clear thesis
- You don't know which of her experiences connects to this topic

**Questions to ask (pick the relevant ones, don't dump all of them):**
- "What's the personal story or experience behind this? Your top posts always have one."
- "Who specifically is this for - someone still in corporate, someone who just left, or someone building?"
- "Is there a specific number, data point, or result you can anchor this around?"
- "What's the one thing you want someone to do or feel after reading this?"
- "Do you want this as one post or should I break it into multiple?"
- "Is there a contrarian angle here? Your best posts challenge a common belief."
- "What happened that made you think about this? The trigger often makes the best hook."

**Don't ask if:**
- The input is a full newsletter or reel script (plenty of material to work with)
- The input already contains a personal story, specific data, and clear thesis
- Hannah has explicitly said what she wants

### Step 3: Choose the Format

Based on the input, select the best post format from `references/post_formats.md`. If the input could yield multiple posts, suggest that and let Hannah decide.

**Format selection logic:**

If the input has a strong personal story or decision -> **Format 1: Personal Story into Lesson**
If the input has 5+ discrete tips, lessons, or items -> **Format 2: Numbered List**
If the input challenges a common belief with evidence -> **Format 3: Hot Take into Reframe**
If the input contains a template, script, or tactical resource -> **Format 4: Resource/Template**
If the input is about someone else's career story -> **Format 5: Someone's Story**

Tell Hannah which format you're using and why, briefly.

### Step 4: Write the Hook

The hook is the most important part. It appears before "see more" (first ~210 characters) and determines whether anyone reads the rest.

**Hannah's proven hook patterns (ranked by performance):**

1. **Contrarian confession** - Say something that sounds wrong but you'll prove right
   - "I'm really glad I worked 100-hour weeks in investment banking."
   - "I quit banking and now tell people to escape the prestige trap. But if I could go back, I'd do it all over again."

2. **Specific result + implied template** - Lead with a concrete outcome
   - "2 out of my 3 job offers last year came from sending cold messages."

3. **Tension/debate hijack** - Enter an existing conversation with a contrarian personal move
   - "The internet is debating whether to take $120k remote or $240k in-office. Meanwhile, I quit my fully remote job to take a pay cut and work in person."

4. **Vulnerable admission + transformation** - Start with what you used to believe/feel
   - "I used to feel embarrassed telling MBA friends and ex banking colleagues that I work in marketing now."

5. **Bold declarative** - State a truth most people haven't articulated
   - "The job you optimized your whole life for might not exist in 2 years."

**Hook checklist (must pass all):**
- Under 210 characters (hard requirement)
- Would make Suzie stop scrolling
- Contains a specific number, credential, or data point when possible
- Challenges a common assumption OR reveals something personal
- No rhetorical questions. Ever.

### Step 5: Write the Body

Follow the chosen format structure from `references/post_formats.md`. Throughout:

- Keep paragraphs to 1-3 sentences, with line breaks between them
- Use bold sparingly for key phrases (not whole sentences)
- Use numbered lists (1️⃣ 2️⃣ or plain numbers), never bullet points
- Use hyphens (-) not em dashes (-)
- Weave personal experience throughout, don't just give advice
- Use specific details: real numbers, real company names, real situations
- Talk TO Suzie ("you") not ABOUT her ("people who")
- Keep total post under 3,000 characters. Aim for 800-1,500.

### Step 6: Write the Closing

Hannah's closings drive engagement. Pick the right type:

- **If the post tells a personal story:** End with a reframe or mindset shift
- **If the post gives tactical advice:** End with a genuine question that invites sharing
- **If the post is a hot take:** End with an open challenge or invitation
- **Optional newsletter plug:** "(wrote more about this in my weekly newsletter!)"
- **Optional sharing CTA:** "Repost for all your friends in tech" - only if natural

### Step 7: Self-Critique

Before presenting the draft, run through this checklist:

**Voice check:**
- Does this sound like Hannah talking to a smart friend?
- Would she actually say these sentences out loud?
- Is there personal story woven in, not just advice?

**AI slop check (scan every sentence):**
- No em dashes (use hyphens)
- No rhetorical questions
- No words from the ban list in `references/voice_and_style.md`
- No present-ing verbs (highlighting, emphasizing, facilitating)
- No hedging (it's worth considering, it's important to note)
- No therapist mode (you're not alone, you're not broken)
- No forced rule-of-three
- No transition questions (the catch? the kicker?)
- No made-up people or examples

**Format check:**
- Hook under 210 characters?
- Total post under 3,000 characters?
- Sweet spot 800-1,500 characters?
- Line breaks between ideas?
- Numbered lists, not bullets?

**Engagement check:**
- Does the hook stop scrolling?
- Specific number, credential, or data point present?
- Would Suzie want to comment or share?

### Step 8: Present the Draft

Show Hannah the post. Include:
1. The post itself (ready to copy-paste into LinkedIn)
2. Character count
3. One sentence on which format you used and why

If you created multiple posts from the same source material, present them separately.

## Repurposing Guidelines

### From Newsletter to LinkedIn

Newsletters are 500-2000 words. LinkedIn posts are 800-1500 characters. You're compressing dramatically.

1. Find the single strongest thesis or story in the newsletter
2. Pull specific data points, quotes, or examples
3. Write a new hook (newsletter openings rarely work as LinkedIn hooks)
4. Build the post around that one thread, not the whole newsletter
5. Add the newsletter plug at the end

A long newsletter might yield 2-3 LinkedIn posts, each pulling a different thread.

### From Reel Script to LinkedIn

Reel scripts are spoken and visual. LinkedIn posts are text-only and scannable.

1. The reel hook often works as a LinkedIn hook (test if it's under 210 chars)
2. Expand on points that were compressed for video
3. Add context that visuals provided in the reel
4. Consider adding a personal story that wasn't in the reel

### From Raw Ideas

Ask questions first (Step 2). Then shape using the format that fits best.

## Multiple Posts from One Source

If the source material is rich enough, suggest splitting into multiple posts. Each should:
- Stand completely alone
- Have its own hook and thesis
- Pull different threads from the source
- Not repeat each other

Tell Hannah: "This could be 2-3 posts. Here's how I'd split it: [brief description of each]. Want all of them or just one?"

## The Golden Rule

Read the draft one final time and ask: "Would Hannah's audience believe she wrote this, or would they suspect AI?"

If there's even a 10% chance they'd suspect AI, rewrite until the answer is zero.
