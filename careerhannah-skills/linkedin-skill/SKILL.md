## Voice First, Everything Else Second

The biggest risk with this skill is producing posts that sound like AI wrote them with Hannah's topics. The goal is the opposite: posts that sound like Hannah wrote them, period.

### What Makes Hannah's LinkedIn Voice Work

Study `references/linkedin_voice_samples.md` deeply. The short version:

**Her hook fingerprint:** Paradox or contradiction first, then explain. Specific numbers. No motivation stated upfront. An implicit "is this crazy?" that invites the reader to judge before understanding.

**Her structural architecture:**
1. Hook: Specific contradiction or observation (50-150 words)
2. Context/Confession: Personal story that explains the hook (100-200 words)
3. Inversion/Reframe: Why conventional wisdom is wrong (100-200 words)
4. Framework/Pattern: The teachable extraction, often with a list (100-300 words)
5. Personal Extension: Brings it back to her, shows she lives this (50-100 words)

The reader pivot happens AFTER personal credibility is established, not before.

**Her sentence-level patterns:**
- Average 16-18 words per sentence, but with wide variation (5-word fragments to 35-word sentences)
- Fragments appear after colons, in cascades, never in opening paragraphs
- Parentheticals do real work (carry core truths, not just asides)
- Uses "But" and "And" to start sentences freely
- Zero filler: no "I think", "in my opinion", "it seems like"
- Uses quotes around words she's redeeming or problematizing: "unserious", "less prestigious", "contentpreneurship"

**Her resonance engine (why people follow):**
- Permission to reject the default path
- A map for non-linear careers that isn't extreme
- An honest insider who admits confusion and cost
- Evidence that different looks better, with receipts

**What her top posts (500K+ impressions) do that lower posts don't:**
- Specificity at scale (exact dollar amounts, class names, study citations)
- Stronger contradictions (more visceral paradoxes)
- Transferable frameworks (not just stories, but extractable principles)
- Challenge the reader's assumptions about themselves
- Include at least one surprising data point

### The Un-Replicable Element (and How to Get Close)

Hannah's credibility comes from admitting past wrong thinking, showing costs alongside benefits, crediting others, updating her narrative, and sharing specific fears. When writing for her, you need her actual experiences, actual numbers, and actual opinions. This is why the interrogation step matters so much.

## Modes

### Mode 1: Write a LinkedIn Post

**Step 1: Assess the Input**

Hannah's input will be one of: raw idea/braindump, newsletter, reel script, bullet points, voice memo transcript, or external article/content.

Read the input. Identify: core thesis, specific stories or data points, which content pillar it maps to, and what's missing.

**Step 2: Topic Quality Gate (Before Writing Anything)**

Before drafting a single word, evaluate whether this topic is worth a LinkedIn post at all. Hannah's top posts share common DNA:

- They have **real stakes** (dollar amounts, career decisions, things that cost her something)
- They **challenge a belief** her audience holds about prestige, careers, or success
- They connect to her **core themes**: ownership vs. salary, nonlinear careers, the prestige trap, building on the side, personal branding for reluctant high-achievers
- They contain a **transferable framework or insight** the reader can apply to their own life
- They have a **genuine paradox** worth exploring (not a manufactured one)

**If the topic fails this gate, push back.** Say something like: "I can write this, but I don't think it's going to hit. Your best posts have real stakes and challenge what your audience believes. This one feels more like thought leadership that sounds smart but doesn't actually say anything new. Can we find a sharper angle, or is there a different topic that's been on your mind?"

A post about productivity gains, generic AI efficiency, or personal workflow optimizations doesn't have the stakes or paradox that drives Hannah's engagement. Her audience doesn't follow her for life hacks. They follow her for permission to reject the default path, a map for building something nonlinear, and honest insider takes with receipts.

**Step 3: Interrogate Hannah (This Is the Skill's Core Value)**

Don't produce mediocre output from thin input. The interrogation is where the post gets good, because only Hannah has the specific details, numbers, and experiences that make her posts feel real.

You're mining for three things:
1. **The specific detail only she has** - exact numbers, real names, real situations, timelines
2. **The contrarian angle** - what does she believe that most people in her audience don't?
3. **The reader connection** - which of her audience's struggles does this touch?

**Always interrogate if:**
- The input is a vague topic with no personal angle
- There's no specific story, data point, or personal experience
- The input could go multiple directions
- The input is under 100 words without a clear thesis
- You don't know which of her experiences connects to this topic

**Questions to mine for specifics (pick 2-3, use AskUserQuestion):**
- "What specific thing happened that made you think about this? The trigger usually makes the best hook."
- "Is there a specific number, dollar amount, or data point here? Your top posts always anchor on one."
- "What's the thing you believe about this that your audience doesn't yet? That's your contrarian angle."
- "Who is this for - someone still in the prestige path, someone who just left, or someone building on the side?"
- "What did this cost you? Your audience trusts you because you show the cost, not just the win."
- "What would you say about this to a smart friend over drinks? Not the polished version - the real one."

**Don't interrogate if:**
- The input is a full newsletter or reel script (plenty of material)
- The input already has a personal story, specific data, and clear thesis
- Hannah explicitly said "just write it"

**Step 4: Choose the Format**

Select from the 5 formats in `references/post_formats.md`. Tell Hannah which and why (one sentence).

**Step 5: Write the Hook**

See `references/hook_techniques.md` and the hook fingerprint analysis in `references/linkedin_voice_samples.md`.

Hannah's highest-performing hooks share these traits:
- Specific number + personal contradiction + prestige challenge
- No motivation stated upfront (never "I want to share" or "let me tell you")
- An implicit invitation to judge before understanding
- Under 210 characters

Generate 3 hook options using different techniques. Present them for Hannah to pick or riff on.

**Step 6: Write the Body**

Follow Hannah's structural architecture (from `references/linkedin_voice_samples.md`):

1. Build personal credibility first, THEN pivot to reader
2. State contradictions upfront, then prove them (she tells you what to think, then shows why)
3. Use specific details: exact dollar amounts, company names, geographies, timelines, class names
4. Parentheticals that carry real weight, not decoration
5. Vary sentence length around emotional peaks (shorter at turning points, longer for context)
6. Use "But" and "And" freely as sentence starters
7. Lists come AFTER context and explanation, never as the main structure
8. When using lists, items are short (3-7 words) and use "→" or "-", not numbered bullets
9. Financial/business terminology woven in naturally (2-3 per post, not constant)
10. Talk TO the reader ("you", "a lot of you are in...") not ABOUT them ("people who")
11. Total post under 3,000 characters. Sweet spot: 800-1,500.

**Step 7: Write the Closing**

Hannah's closing patterns:
- Reframe or mindset shift (not a summary - add something new)
- Genuine question that invites sharing ("what would you add??")
- References to ownership, building, or her newsletter
- Never restates the thesis
- Optional newsletter plug: "(wrote more about this in my weekly newsletter!)"
- Links go in comments, not post body

**Step 8: Voice Check + Humanizer**

Two-pass quality check:

**Pass 1 - Voice Match (against `references/linkedin_voice_samples.md`):**
- Does this sound like Hannah's actual LinkedIn posts, not just her general voice?
- Is there a paradox or contradiction driving the post?
- Are there specific details only she would have?
- Does the reader pivot happen after credibility is established?
- Would her audience believe she wrote this?

**Pass 2 - Slop Scan (against `references/voice_and_style.md`):**
- Scan every sentence against the banned words/phrases list
- Check for: forced contrast ("not X, but Y"), "the real [anything]", "here's what I learned", "stuck with me", "turns out", "something unexpected happened", "what matters is"
- Check for: em dashes, rhetorical questions, -ing corporate verbs, hedging language
- Run the coffee test: read aloud, does it sound like a person talking?

If anything fails either pass, rewrite those parts. Don't present a draft with known issues.

**Step 9: Present the Draft**

Show Hannah:
1. The post itself (ready to copy-paste into LinkedIn)
2. Character count
3. Which format and why (one sentence)
4. Media suggestion (from `references/media_strategy.md`)

## Repurposing Guidelines

### Newsletter -> LinkedIn
Compress dramatically (2000 words -> 1500 characters).

1. Find the single strongest thesis or story
2. Pull specific data points, quotes, examples
3. Write a NEW hook (newsletter openings rarely work on LinkedIn)
4. Build around one thread, not the whole newsletter
5. Newsletter plug at the end
6. A rich newsletter might yield 2-3 posts on different threads

### Reel Script -> LinkedIn
1. Test if the reel hook works as a LinkedIn hook (under 210 chars?)
2. Expand compressed video points
3. Add context that visuals provided
4. Consider adding a personal story not in the reel

### Raw Ideas
Interrogate first (Step 2). Shape using the format that fits.

## Multiple Posts from One Source

If the source is rich, suggest splitting. Each post must:
- Stand alone with its own hook and thesis
- Pull different threads
- Not repeat across posts
- Tell Hannah: "This could be 2-3 posts. Here's how I'd split it."

## The Golden Rules

1. **Voice match over everything.** A post that sounds like Hannah but is 80% polished beats a post that's perfect but sounds like AI.
2. **Interrogate before writing.** The details only Hannah has are what make the post work. Mine for them.
3. **Paradox first.** State the contradiction, then prove it. Hannah's top posts all open with something that sounds wrong.
4. **Specificity is trustworthiness.** If you can name it (dollar amounts, company names, class names, geographies), the reader believes you lived it.
5. **The reader is smart and suspicious.** No handholding. No explaining why they should care. They're already here; you hooked them.
6. **Scrappy > polished.** In writing and media. The more it looks like a person wrote it at midnight, the better it performs.
