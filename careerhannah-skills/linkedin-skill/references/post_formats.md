# LinkedIn Post Formats

Five proven formats. Each has a different structure and serves a different purpose. Pick the one that fits the input material, don't force it.

---

## Format 1: Personal Story into Lesson

**When to use:** Input has a strong personal story, a decision Hannah made, or a specific experience with a clear takeaway.

**Why it works:** Stories are the most natural way to teach. The reader lives the experience with you, then gets the lesson embedded in the narrative rather than preached at them.

**Structure:**
1. Hook: Contrarian confession or vulnerable admission (2 lines)
2. Setup: The situation, with specific details (2-3 short paragraphs)
3. Turning point: What happened, what changed (1-2 paragraphs)
4. Lesson: What you learned, framed as what the READER can take from it (1-2 paragraphs)
5. Close: Reframe or mindset shift. Not a summary - add something new.

**Reader-first check:** The story exists to illuminate something the reader is dealing with. If the story is interesting but the reader doesn't see themselves in it, reframe or pick a different story.

**Character target:** 1,000-1,500

---

## Format 2: Numbered List

**When to use:** Input has 5+ discrete tips, lessons, observations, or items that don't need narrative connective tissue.

**Why it works:** Scannable. Saveable. Shareable. People bookmark lists and send them to colleagues.

**Structure:**
1. Hook: Bold declarative or specific result (2 lines)
2. Brief context: 1-2 sentences on why this list matters to the reader
3. The list: 5-10 items, each 1-2 sentences. Specific and actionable, not vague platitudes.
4. Close: Genuine question that invites the reader to add their own, or a CTA to save/share.

**Reader-first check:** Every item should pass the test: "Would a stranger find this useful right now?" Cut any item that's only relevant to Hannah's specific situation.

**Character target:** 800-1,500

---

## Format 3: Hot Take into Reframe

**When to use:** Input challenges a commonly held belief, and Hannah has evidence or experience that contradicts it.

**Why it works:** Contrarian positions stop the scroll because they create cognitive dissonance. The reader needs to know if you're right.

**Structure:**
1. Hook: Contradiction or bold declarative that states the hot take (2 lines)
2. The common belief: What most people think and why (1-2 paragraphs)
3. The reframe: Why that's wrong, with specific evidence from Hannah's experience (2-3 paragraphs)
4. The nuance: Where the common belief IS true, or the caveats (1 paragraph - this builds credibility)
5. Close: Open challenge or invitation for the reader to consider the reframe.

**Reader-first check:** The hot take should challenge something the reader personally believes or does. If it's a hot take about an industry trend that doesn't affect the reader's daily life, reframe it.

**Character target:** 1,000-1,500

---

## Format 4: Resource/Template

**When to use:** Input contains a tactical framework, template, script, checklist, or step-by-step process the reader can use immediately.

**Why it works:** Highest save rate. People save things they can use later. This format is pure utility.

**Structure:**
1. Hook: Specific result + implied "here's how" (2 lines)
2. Context: 1-2 sentences on the problem this solves
3. The resource: The framework, template, or steps. Formatted clearly with numbers.
4. Brief example: Show it in action with a real or realistic scenario
5. Close: "Save this for later" or "Send this to someone who needs it" - natural, not forced.

**Reader-first check:** The reader should be able to use this resource TODAY without needing to buy anything, hire anyone, or have special access. Pure utility.

**Character target:** 1,000-2,000

---

## Format 5: Someone's Story

**When to use:** Input is about someone else's career journey, decision, or achievement that Hannah finds instructive.

**Why it works:** Third-person stories feel less preachy than direct advice. The reader learns through someone else's experience.

**Structure:**
1. Hook: The most surprising or counterintuitive fact about this person's story (2 lines)
2. Setup: Who this person is, enough context for credibility (1-2 paragraphs)
3. The story: What they did, decided, or built. Specific details. (2-3 paragraphs)
4. The takeaway: What the reader can learn from this, framed as universally applicable (1-2 paragraphs)
5. Close: Tag the person if on LinkedIn. Invite the reader to reflect.

**Reader-first check:** The person's story must be relatable to the reader's world. If it's about a billionaire founder, it won't land for someone making $150K in consulting trying to figure out their next move.

**Character target:** 1,000-1,500

---

## Format Selection Decision Tree

Start here when you're not sure which format to use:

Does the input have a strong personal story? -> **Format 1**
Does the input have 5+ discrete tips or items? -> **Format 2**
Does the input challenge a common belief? -> **Format 3**
Does the input contain a usable framework or template? -> **Format 4**
Is the input about someone else's journey? -> **Format 5**

If the input fits multiple formats, pick the one that best serves the reader. When in doubt, Format 1 (story into lesson) is Hannah's strongest format because her voice shines in narrative.

## Suggesting Multiple Posts

Rich source material (a long newsletter, a topic with many angles) can yield 2-3 posts, each in a different format. For example, a newsletter about career pivots could become:
- Format 1: Hannah's own pivot story
- Format 2: "7 things I learned from pivoting"
- Format 3: Hot take on why most people pivot wrong

Each post must stand alone. Different hooks, different angles, no repeated material.
