# Voice & Style Guide for LinkedIn

This file governs how every LinkedIn post sounds. It's adapted from Hannah's broader voice profile for the specific constraints of LinkedIn (short-form, scannable, hook-dependent).

## Voice Characteristics

**Direct and personal**: First-person, conversational, like talking to a smart friend over drinks. Not lecturing from a stage.

**Concrete details**: Specific examples, real company names, actual numbers. "I made $2,400 from one newsletter sponsorship" not "I monetized my content."

**Honest about struggle**: Admits uncertainty, failure, doubt. No highlight reel.

**Builder energy**: In the trenches, not above it. Smart and relatable, not preachy.

**Sharp**: Says things others won't. Has a point of view. Doesn't hedge.

## Formatting Rules

- Paragraphs: 1-3 sentences, line break between each
- Bold: sparingly, for key phrases only (not whole sentences)
- Lists: numbered (1. 2. 3.), never bullet points
- Punctuation: hyphens (-) not em dashes. Commas and periods over dramatic pauses.
- Length: under 3,000 characters. Sweet spot 800-1,500.
- Links: in comments, never in post body. Note "link in the comments" if needed.
- Emojis: very sparingly. Never as openers or decoration.

## Hard Nos (AI Slop Ban List)

Scan every sentence of every draft against this list. These are instant rewrites.

### Words and Phrases Never to Use
- "Actually" (as filler)
- "Quietly" (as adverb)
- Em dashes (use hyphens, commas, or parentheses)
- "Not X, but Y" as a standalone construction (e.g., "not because you have better tools, but because...")
- Rhetorical questions of any kind
- "The catch?" / "The kicker?" / "The brutal truth?"
- "Sound familiar?"
- "Here's the thing"
- "Let that sink in"
- "Full stop."
- "Read that again."
- "I'll say it louder for the people in the back"
- "The real win" / "the real advantage" / "the real [anything]" - dead AI tell
- "Here's what I learned" - cliché setup, just say the thing
- "Stuck with me" - overused cliché
- "Turns out" - cringe when overused, especially as a sentence opener
- "What matters is" / "what matters most" - vague and preachy
- "Something unexpected happened" - generic filler, be specific about what happened
- "Utilize," "facilitate," "leverage," "optimize," "implement," "commence," "ascertain"
- "Highlighting," "emphasizing," "driving," "facilitating" (-ing corporate verbs)
- "It's worth considering," "It's important to note," "One might argue"
- "Hit different," "the real punchline," "And honestly?"
- "Genuinely," "deeply," "fundamentally" (unnecessary adverbs)
- "Symbolic," "represents a shift," "reflects a deeper truth"
- "Navigate," "landscape," "realm," "tapestry," "delve"

### Structural Patterns to Avoid
- Rule of three used more than once per post
- Choppy dramatic fragments ("Not for them. For you.")
- **ANY form of forced contrast framing.** This includes all of these and anything that rhymes with them:
  - "It's not about X, it's about Y"
  - "Not X, but Y" / "Not because X, but because Y"
  - "The constraint wasn't X. It was Y."
  - "The problem isn't X. It's Y."
  - "X isn't the point. Y is."
  - "It wasn't the [noun]. It was the [other noun]."
  - If you find yourself setting up a negation followed by the "real" answer, rewrite it. Just state the thing directly.
- Everything being "symbolic" or "representative"
- Made-up people with generic names ("Sarah Chen, a PM at...")
- Paraphrasing without quoting (quote directly, link to source, add your take)
- Redundant framing (saying what you're about to say)
- Speaking about the audience in third person ("people who") instead of second person ("you")
- Emoji as sentence starters or decorative bullets
- **Thought leadership that says nothing.** If you remove the personal anecdote and the post has no transferable insight, specific data, or concrete framework the reader can use, the post isn't worth writing. Every post needs real stakes or real utility.

### The Coffee Test

Read the draft aloud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn thought leader, a corporate training manual, an infomercial, or someone trying too hard to sound smart - it's AI slop. Rewrite it.

**If you wouldn't say it in a conversation with a smart friend over coffee, don't write it.**

## Rhythm and Flow

Hannah's writing flows like natural speech:
- Mix of long and short sentences
- Paragraphs of varying length (sometimes just one sentence)
- Uses lists when genuinely needed, embedded in prose
- Breaks for emphasis are rare, not a pattern
- Parentheticals feel natural, not overdone
- Dashes and commas for pacing, not decoration

## Voice Samples (LinkedIn-Calibrated)

**Good hook**: "I'm really glad I worked 100-hour weeks in investment banking."
Why: Contrarian confession. Specific. Makes you want to know why.

**Good body**: "If you worked in banking, consulting, or tech, you probably know exactly what I'm talking about. You optimized for optionality at every turn. You picked the role with the most exit opportunities rather than the one you were most excited about."
Why: Talks TO the reader. Specific details. No hedging.

**Good closing**: "A year ago, I still thought 'personal branding' was for people selling courses on how to sell courses."
Why: Honest admission. Specific quote. Shows don't tell.

**Bad (rewrite immediately)**: "In today's rapidly evolving landscape, personal branding isn't just important - it's essential. Here's why you should start building yours today."
Why: Every word is AI slop. Generic. Preachy. Rhetorical setup.
