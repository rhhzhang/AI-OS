# Hook Techniques for LinkedIn

The hook is the first 2 lines readers see before they click "... more" on LinkedIn. That's roughly 2 short sentences, about 210 characters total. If you don't earn the click, the rest of the post doesn't exist.

## The Job of a Hook

Break the reader's scrolling pattern. That's it. Make them stop, think "wait, what?" and click "see more."

The reader is bored, understimulated, doomscrolling between meetings. They have kids to pick up, emails to send, deadlines to hit. Your hook has about 1.2 seconds to earn their attention. Most hooks die instantly because they sound like everything else in the feed.

## Hook Principles

1. **About the reader or a universal tension.** Never about Hannah bragging. Even when using "I," the reader should see themselves in it.
2. **Create an open loop.** An unanswered question, a contradiction, or a bold claim they can't ignore.
3. **Be specific.** Numbers, credentials, company names. Specificity signals "this is real, not generic advice."
4. **Break a pattern.** Say something that sounds wrong, surprising, or like something a friend would text you that makes you reply "wait, explain."

## Hook Techniques

### 1. Contrarian Confession
Say something that sounds wrong but you'll prove right. The reader thinks "that can't be true" and clicks.

**Hannah's proven examples:**
- "I'm really glad I worked 100-hour weeks in investment banking."
- "I quit banking and now tell people to escape the prestige trap. But if I could go back, I'd do it all over again."

**Formula:** [Surprising personal admission that contradicts expected narrative]

### 2. Specific Result + Implied Template
Lead with a concrete outcome that implies "and you could do this too."

**Hannah's proven examples:**
- "2 out of my 3 job offers last year came from sending cold messages."

**Formula:** [Specific number] + [unexpected method or source]

### 3. Tension/Debate Hijack
Enter an existing conversation with a contrarian personal move.

**Hannah's proven examples:**
- "The internet is debating whether to take $120k remote or $240k in-office. Meanwhile, I quit my fully remote job to take a pay cut and work in person."

**Formula:** [Reference trending debate] + [Your unexpected position]

### 4. Vulnerable Admission + Transformation
Start with what you used to believe or feel. The vulnerability earns trust; the implied change earns the click.

**Hannah's proven examples:**
- "I used to feel embarrassed telling MBA friends and ex banking colleagues that I work in marketing now."

**Formula:** [Honest past belief/feeling] + [implied "and then..."]

### 5. Bold Declarative
State a truth most people haven't articulated. No question mark, no softening.

**Hannah's proven examples:**
- "The job you optimized your whole life for might not exist in 2 years."

**Formula:** [Direct statement that names what people are feeling but not saying]

### 6. Contradiction
Say something that sounds factually wrong. The dissonance forces the click.

**Examples:**
- "The worst LinkedIn posts get the most followers."
- "I stopped trying to go viral. My reach doubled."

**Formula:** [Statement that contradicts common sense about the topic]

### 7. Direct Accusation
Call the reader out on something they know is true but don't want to admit.

**Examples:**
- "You're writing LinkedIn posts for your mom, not your audience."
- "You know your content is boring. So does everyone scrolling past it."

**Formula:** [Point out what the reader is doing wrong, directly]

### 8. Stolen Thought
Say what the reader secretly thinks but won't say out loud.

**Examples:**
- "You've thought about quitting every Sunday night for 6 months. You're not 'just tired.'"
- "Your company's AI strategy is 'wait and see.' That's not a strategy."

**Formula:** [Name the private thought] + [reframe it]

### 9. Absurd Reframe
Take something mundane and make it dramatic through specificity.

**Examples:**
- "Your LinkedIn hook has 1.2 seconds to live. Most die instantly."
- "You spend 40 hours a week making someone else's dream come true. Then you spend 0 hours on yours."

**Formula:** [Mundane thing] + [dramatic specific framing]

### 10. Specific Number + Unexpected Context
Lead with data that creates a "wait, really?" reaction.

**Examples:**
- "I mass-unfollowed 2,000 people. My engagement tripled."
- "344,258 followers in one year. One account. No ads."

**Formula:** [Surprising number] + [unexpected outcome or constraint]

## Hook Generation Process

When generating hooks for Hannah:

1. Understand the post's core thesis and the reader benefit
2. Generate 10 options spread across at least 4 different techniques
3. Write each as the exact 2 lines that would appear on LinkedIn (character count matters)
4. Flag which ones are strongest and why
5. Present via AskUserQuestion so Hannah can pick, combine, or riff

Hannah's taste is the final filter. Claude generates volume; Hannah picks the one that feels right. Over hundreds of posts, her instinct for what works will sharpen.

## What Makes a Hook Fail

- It's about Hannah's achievement with no reader angle
- It's generic enough that anyone could have written it
- It uses a rhetorical question
- It sounds like every other LinkedIn post in the feed
- It doesn't create an open loop (no reason to click "see more")
- It's trying to be clever instead of being specific
- It uses emoji as the opener
- It hedges or qualifies ("I think maybe...")
- It uses forced contrast ("Not because X - because Y") - this is the #1 AI slop pattern in hooks
- It uses "what matters is/what matters most" - vague and preachy
- It relies on "turns out" as a reveal mechanism - overplayed
